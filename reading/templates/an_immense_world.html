Day 119: An Immense World by Ed Yong

Introduction

Ed Yong’s An Immense World: How Animal Senses Reveal the Hidden Realms Around Us is a breathtaking exploration of perception—how animals experience the world in ways wildly different from humans. Published in 2022, this book dives into the concept of umwelt—the unique sensory bubble that each species inhabits—and takes readers on a tour of Earth’s unseen dimensions through the eyes, ears, noses, tongues, and even electric fields of animals.

What makes this book extraordinary is Yong’s lyrical yet scientific storytelling. He doesn’t just explain senses—he immerses us in the alien yet astonishing worlds of other creatures. From bats and whales to beetles and mantis shrimp, An Immense World invites readers to appreciate biodiversity not only in form and behavior, but in perception. It expands our empathy and deepens our curiosity, asking us to rethink how we share this planet.

Summary

An Immense World is structured around different sensory modalities, each chapter focusing on a particular sense and the animal species that master it.

  • Umwelt and Perceptual Worlds – Yong introduces the idea that each animal experiences a filtered version of reality shaped by its sensory tools. There is no single world—only countless versions of it.
  • Scent and Taste – Dogs can detect diseases and track people through smell. Other animals, like snakes and salmon, navigate using chemical trails. Taste, meanwhile, can differ drastically—some animals can taste air or sense with their skin.
  • Sound and Vibration – Bats echolocate, elephants feel seismic vibrations, and whales sing songs that travel vast distances. Soundscapes differ by species.
  • Light and Color – Bees see ultraviolet patterns on flowers, and mantis shrimp perceive colors we can't even imagine. Animals tailor their vision to their needs.
  • Electric and Magnetic Senses – Sharks and eels detect electric fields. Birds and sea turtles navigate using Earth’s magnetic field—senses still not fully understood by science.
  • Pain and Touch – Star-nosed moles have hypersensitive noses, and octopuses feel with their arms. Touch is as varied as sight or smell.
  • Human Impact on Senses – Yong concludes by examining how artificial lights, noise, and pollution are disrupting these delicate sensory worlds, urging us to tread more lightly.

Rather than being a book about animals per se, it’s a book about how animals perceive—a deeply humbling reminder that our view of the world is just one among many.

Memorable Insight or Case Study

One unforgettable story is that of the star-nosed mole, whose bizarre snout is one of the most sensitive touch organs in the animal kingdom. With 22 fleshy appendages, it can “feel” in such fine detail that it recognizes prey in milliseconds—faster than the human eye can process a blink. This insight radically shifts our understanding of tactile perception.

Key Study Points

  • Every Species Lives in Its Own Sensory Bubble – No two animals perceive the world the same way.
  • Humans Are Sensory-Limited – Our five senses are just a tiny sliver of nature’s full toolkit.
  • Sensory Systems Shape Behavior and Evolution – How animals sense determines how they survive.
  • Artificial Environments Harm Animal Perception – Light pollution, noise, and chemicals disrupt vital sensory cues.
  • Empathy Grows from Understanding – Seeing the world through other species’ senses fosters respect and wonder.

Key Concepts and Explanation

  • Umwelt – Coined by ethologist Jakob von Uexküll, it refers to the subjective sensory world of an organism.
    Example: A tick is blind and deaf but can detect body heat and smell sweat—its world is tiny but sufficient.
  • Echolocation – Used by bats and dolphins to “see” with sound.
    Implication: Animals can perceive their environment with astonishing precision without sight.
  • Electroreception – Sensing electric fields, used by fish and some amphibians.
    Relevance: Helps in hunting and navigation, especially in dark or murky environments.
  • Anthropocentrism in Science – The idea that human perception is “normal” or “complete” is flawed.
    Impact: Limits our understanding of animal behavior and sensory ecology.

Quotes

“The world is full of sights and sounds and smells and textures that humans can’t perceive.”
“To understand the umwelt of another creature is to peer into a parallel universe.”

Fun or Surprising Fact

Some butterflies have taste sensors on their feet, allowing them to land on a leaf and instantly know whether it's nutritious. Their “feet” do the job of a human tongue—an elegant example of nature’s sensory creativity.

Real-Life Application

The book encourages light- and noise-conscious city planning, preservation of natural soundscapes, and deeper respect for animals’ sensory needs in zoos, farms, and conservation areas. It also inspires a personal shift—asking us to be quieter, dimmer, and more attentive to the invisible wonders around us.

Recommended Books

  • Are We Smart Enough to Know How Smart Animals Are? by Frans de Waal – Explores animal cognition and how our biases cloud our judgment.
  • The Soul of an Octopus by Sy Montgomery – A beautiful meditation on the intelligence and inner world of a remarkable sea creature.
