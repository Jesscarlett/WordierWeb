Day 255: Being a Beast by Charles Foster

Introduction

Being a Beast by Charles Foster, published in 2016, is one of the most unusual and thought-provoking nature books of recent years. It’s part memoir, part immersive journalism, part philosophical inquiry—and completely unlike any traditional book about animals.

Foster, a British writer, barrister, and veterinarian, sets out to understand animals not by observing them from afar, but by trying to live as they do. He digs dens, eats worms, sniffs scents, and crawls naked through the woods—all in an effort to answer the question: what is it like to be a wild animal?

The result is a funny, profound, and deeply personal meditation on perception, identity, wildness, and the limits of human understanding.

Living Like a Beast

The premise of Being a Beast is simple and wild: to understand animals, Foster attempts to live as one. He chooses five species—badger, otter, fox, red deer, and swift—and devotes himself to experiencing life as each of them.

This means:

  • Living in a hillside den like a badger.
  • Swimming rivers and catching fish with his teeth like an otter.
  • Dumpster diving in London alleyways like a fox.
  • Roaming the Scottish Highlands in near silence like a red deer.
  • Trying to live in constant motion and migration like a swift.

Foster takes this method acting approach to the extreme—crawling on all fours, eating roadkill, sleeping outdoors, and temporarily giving up human comforts and perspectives.

It’s eccentric, often uncomfortable, but always sincere.

The Badger: Sensory Overload

Foster begins with the badger. He and his son dig a burrow in the Welsh countryside and try to live like nocturnal foragers, sniffing out worms in the soil.

This section becomes an exploration of smell. Badgers, like many animals, experience the world primarily through scent. Foster, by contrast, realizes how dulled and underused his own sense of smell has become. He tries to “see” the landscape through scent trails, teaching himself to notice the invisible layers of information that humans usually ignore.

Living underground and adopting a badger's sensory priorities leads Foster to question how we define reality at all—if perception creates our world, then what is the human world missing?

The Otter: Fluid Freedom

For the otter, Foster dives into rivers and streams, practicing how to swim with flow and grace, immersing himself in the fluid environment that otters inhabit.

He writes about otters as beings of constant motion, of water and play, living in a world without borders. While humans construct barriers and territory, otters glide through a dimension that is harder to control or divide.

This chapter becomes a meditation on freedom, adaptability, and sensory openness. But it also highlights Foster’s physical limitations. He can’t see underwater clearly, he can’t fish with his teeth, and he nearly gets hypothermia.

The otter reminds him—and us—that full understanding may be impossible, but the effort reveals deeper truths.

The Fox: Urban Wildness

To become a fox, Foster turns to the city. Foxes thrive in human landscapes, scavenging, sneaking, surviving. He wanders the night streets of London, sleeping in alleyways, raiding bins for food, and learning to blend into the background.

This section is both urban anthropology and an investigation of marginality—what it means to live on the edges of society, to be invisible but present.

Foster finds unexpected kinship with the fox: cunning, restless, half-wild, half-domestic. The fox is an animal that navigates two worlds—just like Foster, who is both a scholar and a seeker, a human trying to touch wildness.

The Red Deer: Prey Mindset

Living as a red deer in the Scottish Highlands, Foster tries to experience the life of a prey animal.

He focuses on silence, caution, and fear—spending hours in stillness, listening for predators, suppressing his human urge to speak or act.

This chapter becomes a psychological deep dive into vulnerability. Deer live in a constant state of alertness, tuned to danger in ways humans have forgotten. Foster tries to reawaken those instincts.

He describes the internal transformation of becoming prey—not just playing the role, but letting fear and attentiveness reshape his perception of the world.

The Swift: The Unreachable

Finally, Foster reflects on the swift—a bird that spends nearly its entire life in the air, eating, sleeping, mating, and migrating in flight.

Here, Foster admits he cannot truly live like a swift. He cannot fly, cannot experience the wind-borne sensory world they inhabit. This chapter becomes more philosophical, less physical—a recognition of the limits of empathy and embodiment.

But rather than see that as failure, Foster views it as awe. Some creatures remain unreachable, unknowable—and that mystery is part of what makes them beautiful.

Why This Book Matters

Being a Beast is not a traditional nature book. It doesn’t offer data sets or structured observations. Instead, it offers vulnerability, imagination, and an honest attempt to dissolve the boundary between human and animal.

Foster’s writing is lyrical, intellectual, and often funny. His willingness to look foolish in the pursuit of understanding is both humbling and inspiring.

The book ultimately argues that true understanding requires more than just analysis—it requires embodiment, empathy, and the willingness to surrender certainty.

Key Takeaways and Insights

  • Animals experience the world through senses humans often neglect—like scent, motion, and magnetic fields.
  • To understand another species, we must rethink what it means to be “rational” or “intelligent.”
  • Full understanding may be impossible—but the act of trying reveals deep insights into perception and selfhood.
  • The boundaries between human and animal are not fixed—they can be blurred through attention and intention.
  • Being wild is not about chaos—it’s about deep attunement to place, presence, and instinct.
  • Living as an animal—even imperfectly—can teach us to be better humans: more humble, more present, more alive.
