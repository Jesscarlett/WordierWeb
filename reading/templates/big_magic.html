Day 20: Big Magic by Elizabeth Gilbert

Introduction

Big Magic: Creative Living Beyond Fear by Elizabeth Gilbert is a guide to living a creative life free from the constraints of fear and self-doubt. Known for her bestselling memoir *Eat, Pray, Love*, Gilbert offers readers practical advice, personal stories, and insights into the creative process. In *Big Magic*, she explores the notion that creativity is not only for artists, but for anyone looking to live a fuller, more authentic life. The book encourages individuals to pursue their creative passions without waiting for the perfect circumstances or permission, and instead to trust the process and embrace the magic of creativity.

Summary

Big Magic is divided into six key sections that cover different aspects of living a creative life. Each section provides guidance on overcoming the fears and obstacles that often hold people back from following their creative passions.

Part One: Courage

In the first section, Gilbert emphasizes that creativity requires courage. She encourages readers to embrace their creativity, even in the face of fear. Fear often acts as a barrier that keeps us from fully engaging in creative pursuits, and Gilbert argues that we should not wait for fear to go away before we start creating. Instead, she urges readers to move forward with their creative endeavors despite fear. Creativity, she suggests, is not a rare gift given to a few exceptional people but a process that everyone can engage in, regardless of their background or circumstances.

Part Two: Enchantment

Gilbert describes creativity as a form of magic—something enchanting and mysterious that has the power to surprise and delight. She talks about the "muse" or "genius" as an energy or force that interacts with the creator, guiding and inspiring them to create. According to Gilbert, creativity is not something that comes purely from within the individual, but is a collaborative process with the universe. She encourages readers to open themselves up to the possibility of magic in their creative lives, and to trust in the process even when the outcome is uncertain.

Part Three: Permission

This section discusses the importance of giving yourself permission to be creative. Gilbert argues that many people limit themselves by waiting for permission from external sources—whether from family, society, or other creators. She urges readers to stop seeking approval and to instead create because it is something they enjoy and feel called to do. Gilbert emphasizes that creativity does not need to be justified by external recognition or success. The act of creating itself is valuable, and every person has the right to create, regardless of the end result.

Part Four: Persistence

Persistence is a crucial element in the creative process, and Gilbert discusses how to cultivate it. Creativity is not always glamorous or easy, and the road to success is often filled with failure, rejection, and setbacks. Gilbert shares her own struggles with the writing process and how perseverance through those challenges helped her achieve her creative goals. She encourages readers to stick with their creative pursuits, even when the results do not immediately meet their expectations. The key to success, she argues, is continuing to show up and put in the work, regardless of external validation.

Part Five: Trust

Gilbert explains that trust is essential in the creative process. Trusting that the work is worth doing, trusting the process, and trusting oneself are all crucial aspects of creativity. She discusses the importance of trusting that the right ideas and opportunities will come when they are meant to, and that the creative process is often unpredictable. Gilbert also touches on the importance of trusting that the universe or the muse will provide inspiration and guidance when needed. She urges readers to trust in their own abilities and to believe that the work they do is important, even if it is not always recognized or celebrated.

Part Six: Divinity

In the final section, Gilbert explores the concept of creative living as a spiritual practice. She discusses how creativity can be an expression of the divine, and how living a creative life can be a path to personal fulfillment and deeper connection with the universe. Creativity, according to Gilbert, is a way of expressing who we are and how we interact with the world around us. She encourages readers to see creativity as a gift and to approach it with gratitude and reverence. Gilbert concludes by reaffirming that living a creative life is an act of bravery and self-love, and that we all have the potential to tap into this energy and create something meaningful.

Story

One of the most memorable stories in *Big Magic* is about Gilbert’s own experience with fear and creativity. She recounts how, after the success of *Eat, Pray, Love*, she faced a tremendous amount of pressure to write another book that would live up to her previous success. She struggled with self-doubt and the fear of failure but ultimately chose to push through that fear and write her next book, *The Signature of All Things*. This story illustrates the central message of the book: that creative living requires overcoming fear and continuing to create, regardless of the outcome or external expectations.

Key Study Points

  • Embrace Fear: Fear is a natural part of the creative process, but it should not stop you from pursuing your passions. Creativity and fear can coexist, and the key is to move forward despite fear.
  • Creativity is for Everyone: Creativity is not reserved for a select few. Anyone can engage in the creative process, and it is a tool for personal growth and self-expression.
  • Trust the Process: Creativity is not always predictable, but trusting the process and having faith in the flow of ideas is essential. Creativity often comes when least expected.
  • Give Yourself Permission: Stop waiting for permission to be creative. Create because it brings you joy, not because you need external approval or validation.
  • Persevere Through Setbacks: Creativity requires persistence. There will be failure and rejection along the way, but persistence and continued effort are key to success.
  • Creativity is Spiritual: Gilbert views creativity as a spiritual practice, something that connects us to something larger than ourselves. By embracing creativity, we connect to the divine energy of life.

Key Concepts and Explanation

  • Big Magic: The title refers to the idea that creativity is a magical, enchanting process that can transform lives. Gilbert suggests that creativity is a gift available to everyone, and we should embrace it without fear or hesitation.
  • The Muse: Gilbert describes the muse as a force or energy that inspires and guides creativity. The muse is not something we control, but something we interact with when we choose to engage in the creative process.
  • Fear vs. Creativity: Fear is a natural obstacle that prevents many people from fully engaging in creativity. Gilbert teaches that fear is not something to eliminate, but something to coexist with while pursuing creative endeavors.

Quotes

  • "Creativity is not a talent. It is a way of operating."
  • "You do not need permission to live a creative life."
  • "You can’t use up creativity. The more you use, the more you have."
  • "The universe buries strange jewels deep within us all, and then stands back to see if we can find them."

Fun or Surprising Fact

Big Magic was inspired by a TED Talk Elizabeth Gilbert gave in 2009, which became one of the most popular TED Talks of all time. The book was a natural extension of her thoughts on creativity, and it went on to become a bestseller, resonating with millions of people around the world.

Real-Life Application

The lessons in *Big Magic* can be applied to anyone looking to live a more creative and fulfilling life. By embracing fear, giving yourself permission to create, and trusting the process, you can unlock your full creative potential. Gilbert’s approach to creativity can be used not just for artists, but for anyone looking to bring more passion, joy, and authenticity into their life. Whether you’re writing, painting, building a business, or pursuing a personal goal, the principles in *Big Magic* encourage you to live boldly and creatively.

Recommended Books

  • Big Magic: Creative Living Beyond Fear by Elizabeth Gilbert – A practical and inspiring guide to creative living.
  • The War of Art by Steven Pressfield – A book about overcoming the resistance that prevents us from doing our creative work.
