Day 133: Breath: The New Science of a Lost Art by James Nestor

Introduction

Breathing is the most essential function of life—and yet most of us are doing it wrong. In Breath: The New Science of a Lost Art, science journalist James Nestor explores how modern habits and lifestyle have damaged the way we breathe, and how reclaiming ancient techniques can transform our health, performance, and well-being.

Published in 2020, Breath is part memoir, part scientific investigation, and part practical guide. With an engaging mix of personal experimentation, interviews with experts, and historical research, Nestor takes readers on a fascinating journey to rediscover the power of the breath.

Summary

Nestor begins with a striking observation: mouth breathing has become increasingly common, and it’s wreaking havoc on our bodies. From crooked teeth and poor sleep to anxiety and asthma, improper breathing has far-reaching consequences.

To test this, Nestor partners with researchers and undergoes a radical experiment: he blocks his nose for 10 days and breathes only through his mouth. The results are alarming—his blood pressure spikes, his sleep worsens, and his mental clarity declines. When he switches to nasal breathing, the changes are just as dramatic—his health rapidly improves.

The book spans the science and history of breath. Ancient cultures like the Greeks, Hindus, and Chinese developed sophisticated breathing techniques (pranayama, qigong, etc.) to control the breath for better health. Modern research is now catching up to these ancient insights.

Nestor explores different methods of breathwork, including Buteyko, Wim Hof, coherent breathing, and Tummo breathing. He also investigates the benefits of slow breathing, nose breathing, and extending exhalations to reduce stress and improve resilience.

Ultimately, Breath shows that we can heal and enhance nearly every system in our body—just by changing how we inhale and exhale.

Core Concept

The core thesis of Breath is simple but powerful: how you breathe affects every aspect of your health—and most of us are doing it wrong. Fast, shallow, mouth-based breathing triggers stress responses, worsens sleep, and undermines physical performance.

By contrast, breathing through the nose, slowly and deeply, activates the parasympathetic nervous system, improves oxygen efficiency, balances carbon dioxide levels, and supports mental and emotional stability.

In short, how you breathe matters as much as what you eat or how you move. Correcting your breath can be one of the most affordable, accessible, and overlooked ways to transform your well-being.

Key Insights and Strategies

  • Mouth Breathing Is Harmful: Breathing through the mouth, especially during sleep, leads to reduced oxygen absorption, snoring, dental problems, and even facial deformities over time.
  • Nasal Breathing Enhances Performance: The nose filters, humidifies, and regulates airflow. It also releases nitric oxide, a molecule that improves circulation and kills pathogens.
  • Slower Is Better: The ideal breathing rate is about 5.5 breaths per minute. Slower breathing improves heart rate variability, calms the nervous system, and lowers blood pressure.
  • Carbon Dioxide Is Not the Enemy: We tend to over-breathe and flush out CO₂, which is essential for proper oxygen delivery. Tolerating higher CO₂ levels can improve endurance and respiratory efficiency.
  • Breath Can Be a Tool for Healing: Techniques like Buteyko (which emphasizes breath retention) and Tummo (which uses breath to generate inner heat) have therapeutic value for anxiety, asthma, autoimmune conditions, and more.

Quotes to Remember

“No matter what you eat, how much you exercise, how skinny or young or wise you are, none of it matters if you’re not breathing properly.”
“The missing pillar in health is breath.”
“The way we breathe now is a cause of many of our chronic health problems, not a solution.”

Practical Application

  • Breathe through your nose—day and night. Try mouth taping at night if needed.
  • Slow your breathing—aim for around 5–6 breaths per minute.
  • Practice breath-holds—to improve CO₂ tolerance and calm the nervous system.
  • Use extended exhalations—to activate the parasympathetic state and reduce anxiety.
  • Explore breathwork techniques—such as box breathing, Buteyko, or alternate nostril breathing.

These practices are free, simple, and can be integrated into your daily routine in minutes. From athletes to stressed-out office workers, breathing well is universally transformative.

Impact and Reception

Breath was an instant bestseller and resonated with a wide audience—from yogis to scientists to everyday readers. Its accessible writing, grounded in both personal narrative and academic research, made the book a gateway into the exploding field of breathwork.

It has inspired a renewed interest in ancient breathing practices and prompted health professionals to take respiratory habits more seriously. The book also contributed to mainstream awareness about the dangers of mouth breathing and the importance of sleep quality, dental development, and nasal health.
