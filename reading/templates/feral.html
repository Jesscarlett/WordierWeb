Day 215: Feral by George Monbiot

Introduction

In a world dominated by control, structure, and ecological decline, Feral by journalist and activist George Monbiot offers something rare and radical: the idea that we must let go to truly heal.

Part memoir, part manifesto, Feral is a passionate, provocative argument for rewilding—not just of landscapes, but of human lives. Monbiot challenges us to abandon the illusion of mastery over nature and embrace its unpredictability, complexity, and vitality.

This is not just a book about conservation. It’s about liberation.

What is Rewilding, Really?

At its core, rewilding is the act of restoring ecosystems to their natural state by removing human intervention and reintroducing species that once belonged.

But Monbiot expands the definition. Rewilding, to him, is as much about reconnecting with our own wildness—breaking free from the sterile routines of modern life and rediscovering awe, adventure, and humility in the face of nature.

He recounts his own restlessness—a sense of being domesticated, dulled by city life and environmental doom—and how rewilding offered not just ecological hope, but personal renewal.

The Problem with Conservation Today

Much of modern conservation, Monbiot argues, is too small-minded. It focuses on preservation rather than restoration, on managing the status quo rather than transforming it.

We protect degraded landscapes as if they’re ideal. We build fences. We obsess over endangered species while ignoring the broken ecosystems they inhabit.

Monbiot takes aim at this narrow vision. He asks: why are we so afraid to imagine what nature could be, if given space to flourish?

He believes we’ve forgotten what wildness really looks like—and how exhilarating, dynamic, and vital it can be.

A Landscape of Loss

Feral takes readers on a journey across Britain’s landscapes—bleak uplands, overgrazed hillsides, and drained wetlands. Monbiot paints a stark picture: vast areas that appear “natural” are, in fact, biologically impoverished.

Centuries of farming, especially sheep grazing, have stripped these areas of forests, predators, and biodiversity. The result is what he calls “sheepwrecked” terrain: green, treeless, and empty.

He calls for an ecological awakening—not just to protect what’s left, but to restore what’s been lost.

The Case for Big Ideas

Monbiot makes a compelling case for ambitious rewilding.

This includes reintroducing lost species—like beavers, lynxes, and even wolves—who play key roles in maintaining healthy ecosystems. He argues that these animals are not dangerous intruders but vital actors in the drama of life.

Wolves, for instance, regulate deer populations, which allows forests to regrow. Beavers create wetlands that support fish, amphibians, and birds.

Monbiot is not naïve about the challenges. He addresses the political and cultural resistance to reintroducing predators. But he insists: if we want real change, we must be bold.

The Sea: A Forgotten Frontier

One of Feral’s most powerful sections focuses on the ocean.

Monbiot describes diving into waters once teeming with fish, now eerily silent and empty. He laments the destruction of marine ecosystems from trawling, dredging, and overfishing.

But he also offers hope.

Marine reserves—areas where no fishing is allowed—can rebound dramatically. Within just a few years, fish populations explode, coral returns, and biodiversity thrives.

He urges governments to create large, enforced no-take zones to allow the sea to heal. The ocean, like the land, has the power to regenerate—if only we allow it.

Rewilding the Self

Beyond ecology, Monbiot argues for a rewilding of the human spirit.

He shares personal stories of kayaking along remote coasts, sleeping under the stars, and finding wonder in unexpected places. These moments remind us that wildness is not just “out there”—it’s within us.

Modern life, he argues, has narrowed our emotional and imaginative range. We are safer, richer, and more connected than ever, yet often feel detached, anxious, and unfulfilled.

Rewilding, then, is a remedy. It’s about breaking routines, confronting the unknown, and rekindling a sense of belonging to something larger than ourselves.

Quotes to Remember

“Rewilding is not about abandoning civilization but about enhancing it. It is to ‘love not man the less, but Nature more.’”
“Ecological boredom is a disease of the mind, born of familiarity and limitation.”
“You can’t know the wild until you let it take you.”

A Vision for the Future

Monbiot’s vision is unapologetically ambitious. He imagines landscapes where rivers meander freely, apex predators roam, and people live alongside wildness—not in opposition to it.

He calls for policies that reward ecological restoration, not just preservation. He encourages individuals to support rewilding projects, challenge destructive land use, and advocate for a wilder, richer future.

And he insists that rewilding is not nostalgic—it’s revolutionary. It is a forward-looking, evidence-based strategy that can revive not just ecosystems, but human hope.

Why This Book Matters

In an age where environmental despair is common, Feral offers something rare: a vision filled with possibility.

George Monbiot doesn’t pretend rewilding is easy. He knows it’s controversial, uncertain, and slow. But he argues that it’s worth it. Because the alternative—managed decline—is no longer enough.

Whether you’re a nature lover, a policymaker, or just someone seeking a more meaningful connection to the world, Feral challenges you to dream bigger.

It dares you to imagine a world where wildness isn’t the enemy of civilization—but its salvation.
