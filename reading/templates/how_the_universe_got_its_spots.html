Day 192: How the Universe Got Its Spots by Janna Levin

Introduction

What if the universe isn’t infinite?

What if, like the surface of a globe, space loops back on itself in a vast cosmic shape?

In How the Universe Got Its Spots, astrophysicist Janna Levin explores one of the most mind-bending questions in cosmology: what is the shape, structure, and ultimate size of the universe?

But this book isn’t just a science treatise. It’s also a memoir—a personal, poetic journey told through letters Levin writes to her mother while grappling with the math, mysteries, and messiness of living with a mind full of space.

Blending astrophysics with reflections on love, time, and existence, this book invites readers not just to understand the cosmos—but to feel it.

The Premise: Cosmic Topology

Levin’s central question is deceptively simple: What is the shape of the universe?

We tend to think of space as infinite. But what if it’s not? What if, like the surface of a donut or a three-dimensional video game map, the universe wraps around itself?

This idea belongs to a branch of mathematics called topology—the study of shapes and their properties. A key insight is that space could be finite but unbounded. That means you could travel forever without hitting an edge, because space would fold back on itself.

In such a universe, light could loop around the cosmos, and you might see multiple images of the same galaxy—or even your own planet—in different directions.

The “spots” in the book’s title refer to the cosmic microwave background—the afterglow of the Big Bang. These tiny fluctuations in temperature and density may hold the key to discovering the true topology of the universe.

Levin’s scientific goal is to decode these patterns and see if they reveal a repeating, folded universe.

Narrative Highlights

This book is not structured like a textbook. It’s more like a diary. Levin writes to her mother from various locations—London, Cambridge, New York—sharing her thoughts on work, relationships, and the cosmos.

This format makes the science feel intimate and accessible. Levin writes about:

  • The Infinite vs. the Finite – The emotional and intellectual impact of trying to imagine an infinite cosmos versus one that loops back on itself.
  • The Spots – How tiny temperature differences in the cosmic background radiation offer clues about the universe’s curvature and boundaries.
  • The Shape of Space – From flat to spherical to saddle-shaped to more exotic “multiply connected” geometries.
  • Science and Emotion – Levin openly discusses her loneliness, her desire for connection, and her awe at the immensity of what she studies.
  • The Fragility of Knowledge – She reflects on how science is provisional, full of errors, and constantly evolving—as are people.

Core Scientific Concepts

  • Topology of the Universe – Studying the shape and size of the cosmos beyond just whether it's expanding. Can it loop around itself? Can we detect a repeating pattern in space?
  • Cosmic Microwave Background (CMB) – The residual heat from the Big Bang, spread across the sky. Its “spots” contain information about the universe’s early structure and overall geometry.
  • Flat vs. Curved Space – Depending on the total mass and energy, the universe could be flat (Euclidean), positively curved (spherical), or negatively curved (saddle-shaped).
  • Multiply Connected Space – A universe that folds in on itself. Like a video game level where walking off one side makes you appear on the other.
  • Observational Cosmology – Using satellites like WMAP and COBE (and later Planck) to detect temperature variations in the CMB and infer cosmic structure.

Personal and Philosophical Dimensions

What sets this book apart is Levin’s willingness to get personal.

She writes about being in love and being alone. About the stress of scientific deadlines and the pressure of intellectual ambition. About feeling simultaneously humbled and elevated by the universe’s vastness.

There’s a poignant moment when she reflects on whether the universe is infinite, and what that says about us. Is human life insignificant in an endless cosmos? Or more precious because we have the ability to ask these questions?

Levin invites readers into her world—not just the equations on her whiteboard, but the thoughts that keep her up at night.

Quotes to Remember

“Infinity is a terrifying prospect. I think it’s a cop-out. I think it's easier to allow for infinity than to work out the details of a closed space.”
“What if the universe were small enough for us to detect its shape?”
“I love the idea of a finite universe. It makes me feel safe, as though I live in something that can be understood.”
“I’m caught between the pull of reason and the tug of intuition.”

Key Takeaways

  • The universe might not be infinite—it could be a closed shape with a specific size and structure.
  • Cosmic background radiation is a key observational tool for discovering the universe’s geometry.
  • Science and emotion are not opposites. Wonder, loneliness, and beauty are all part of the scientific journey.
  • Asking big questions about the universe helps us understand our small but significant place within it.
  • Levin’s unique voice shows that science can be lyrical, personal, and deeply human.

Conclusion

How the Universe Got Its Spots is a love letter—to the cosmos, to science, and to the human desire for understanding.

It’s a book for people who love ideas, who enjoy thinking about space and time while walking through the city or staring out a window.

Janna Levin’s blend of science and soul makes this a standout in the genre. It’s a book that teaches without lecturing, that explores without simplifying, and that connects the vastness of the universe to the interior world of a curious mind.

This is cosmology as conversation. Personal. Honest. Infinite in its wonder.
