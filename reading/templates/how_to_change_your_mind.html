Day 128: How to Change Your Mind by Michael Pollan

Introduction

How to Change Your Mind: What the New Science of Psychedelics Teaches Us About Consciousness, Dying, Addiction, Depression, and Transcendence is Michael Pollan’s revolutionary exploration of the modern psychedelic renaissance. Published in 2018, the book dives into the science, history, and personal experience of substances like LSD, psilocybin (magic mushrooms), and ayahuasca.

What sets this book apart is Pollan’s role as a curious, skeptical outsider. Known for writing about food and nature, he now turns his journalistic eye inward, blending scientific reporting with his own transformative psychedelic journeys. The result is a powerful synthesis of neuroscience, psychology, mysticism, and memoir. The book reignited mainstream interest in psychedelics, helped shift public perception, and supported calls for research-based policy reform.

Summary

The book is structured into six major parts:

  • A Renaissance – Pollan introduces the “psychedelic renaissance,” highlighting recent clinical studies and the history of how psychedelics were criminalized. He outlines how these substances are now being revisited by neuroscientists and therapists for their potential to treat depression, anxiety, addiction, and existential distress.
  • Natural History: The Mushroom – Pollan dives into the world of psilocybin mushrooms, tracing their cultural history and biology. He interviews mushroom hunters, underground guides, and ethnobotanists.
  • History: The First Wave – This section explores the rise and fall of psychedelics in the 1950s–1970s. Figures like Albert Hofmann, Timothy Leary, and Richard Alpert (Ram Dass) take center stage as Pollan reflects on how cultural backlash halted promising research.
  • Travelogue: The New Psychonauts – Pollan undergoes guided psychedelic sessions with LSD, psilocybin, and 5-MeO-DMT, describing mystical experiences, ego dissolution, and emotional catharsis.
  • The Neuroscience – He explores how psychedelics reduce activity in the default mode network (DMN)—the brain’s center for self-referential thought—and explains how this “reset” can help people break free from mental illness.
  • Therapeutic Uses – Pollan profiles clinical trials and patients who’ve overcome addiction, PTSD, and fear of death through psychedelic therapy, especially in palliative care.

Memorable Insight or Case Study

One deeply moving case is that of a terminal cancer patient who took psilocybin during a clinical trial. Her overwhelming fear of death was replaced with a sense of connection and peace. She said it felt like “merging with the universe,” and her remaining months were filled with acceptance and even joy. This case encapsulates the book’s thesis: psychedelics can help reframe our deepest existential anxieties.

Key Study Points

  • Psychedelics Reduce the Grip of the Ego – By quieting the DMN, they promote a sense of unity, peace, and expanded awareness.
  • They May Help with Mental Illness – Clinical trials show promise for depression, addiction, and trauma.
  • Set and Setting Matter – The mindset and environment during a trip heavily influence the experience.
  • History Shows Both Promise and Pitfall – Once hailed as miracle drugs, psychedelics were derailed by politics and misuse.
  • Mystical Experience Has Therapeutic Value – Feelings of awe, ego-loss, and connection have lasting psychological benefits.

Key Concepts and Explanation

  • Default Mode Network (DMN) – A network of brain regions linked to ego and self-referential thinking.
    Effect: Psychedelics lower DMN activity, leading to ego dissolution.
  • Neuroplasticity – Psychedelics enhance the brain’s ability to form new connections.
    Result: Rigid thought patterns (e.g., in depression) become more flexible.
  • Ego Dissolution – The temporary loss of self-boundaries.
    Impact: Promotes feelings of interconnectedness and transcendence.
  • Guided Therapy – Controlled, therapist-led sessions can safely harness psychedelic effects.
    Framework: Combines ancient rituals with modern psychology.

Quotes

“The psychedelic experience is, among other things, a journey to the roots of the self.”
“To change the mind, one must lose it—temporarily.”

Fun or Surprising Fact

Psychedelics like LSD were once studied by major pharmaceutical companies and tested on thousands of patients in the 1950s and ‘60s—including Alcoholics Anonymous co-founder Bill Wilson—before the War on Drugs halted nearly all research for decades.

Real-Life Application

The book encourages more open-mindedness about altered states of consciousness—not for recreation, but for healing, growth, and spiritual insight. It also calls for drug policy reform based on science, not stigma, and highlights the potential of psychedelic therapy as a legitimate mental health treatment.

Recommended Books

  • The Doors of Perception by Aldous Huxley – A classic account of a mescaline trip and its philosophical implications.
  • The Psychedelic Explorer’s Guide by James Fadiman – A practical and historical guide to safe and meaningful psychedelic use.
