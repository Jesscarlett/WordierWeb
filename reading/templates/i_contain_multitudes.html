Day 118: I Contain Multitudes by Ed Yong

Introduction

Ed Yong’s I Contain Multitudes: The Microbes Within Us and a Grander View of Life is a brilliantly written journey into the microscopic world that lives in and around us. Published in 2016, the book unveils how bacteria and other microbes are not merely germs to be eradicated, but essential partners in our survival, development, behavior, and evolution.

What distinguishes this book is Yong’s ability to make microbiology accessible, even poetic. With clarity, wit, and infectious enthusiasm, he explores how our microbiome shapes everything from digestion and immunity to mental health and reproduction. The title, inspired by Walt Whitman, reflects Yong’s central message: we are not single organisms, but ecosystems—each of us containing multitudes.

Summary

I Contain Multitudes is structured around the theme of symbiosis and explores the astonishing roles that microbes play across the living world.

  • Reframing Microbes – Yong begins by challenging the germophobic mindset. While microbes can cause disease, the vast majority are beneficial or harmless, and we couldn’t survive without them.
  • Microbes Across Species – From deep-sea tubeworms to corals, from squids to koalas, Yong shows that microbes are fundamental to the life processes of countless species. Some microbes provide light, digest toxins, or enable reproduction.
  • The Human Microbiome – He explains how trillions of microbes influence our health, immunity, metabolism, and even brain function. Microbial imbalances are linked to conditions like allergies, obesity, and anxiety.
  • Birth and Development – Babies inherit microbes during birth and breastfeeding, shaping their immune systems and gut health. Our microbial companions begin their influence from our first breath.
  • The Future of Microbial Science – Yong examines fecal transplants, probiotics, and microbiome engineering, offering both hope and caution. The field is promising but still young and complex.

By the end, the reader sees life as an interconnected web, where microbes are not separate from us—but part of us.

Memorable Insight or Case Study

A memorable case is the bobtail squid, which glows in the dark thanks to a single bacterial species in its light organ. The squid recruits these bacteria shortly after birth. Without them, it’s vulnerable to predators. This beautifully illustrates symbiosis—how life, even at the smallest scale, thrives in collaboration.

Key Study Points

  • Microbes Are Essential to Life – They influence health, development, and evolution.
  • Symbiosis Is the Rule, Not the Exception – Every animal is a partnership with microbes.
  • The Microbiome Affects the Brain – Gut microbes influence mood, behavior, and cognition.
  • Hygiene and Medicine Need Rethinking – Not all bacteria are bad; overuse of antibiotics harms us.
  • We Are Superorganisms – Human bodies are colonies of human and microbial cells working together.

Key Concepts and Explanation

  • Microbiome – The community of microorganisms living in and on an organism.
    Example: The human gut hosts trillions of bacteria that digest food and produce nutrients.
  • Symbiosis – Close, long-term interactions between species.
    Types: Mutualism (beneficial), commensalism (neutral), parasitism (harmful).
  • Vertical vs. Horizontal Transmission – Microbes can be passed from parent to offspring or between individuals/environment.
    Significance: Impacts co-evolution and disease spread.
  • Probiotics and Prebiotics – Probiotics add beneficial bacteria; prebiotics feed them.
    Caution: Many commercial products lack scientific backing.

Quotes

“The animal kingdom would be nothing without the microbial world.”
“We are not individuals. We are ecosystems.”

Fun or Surprising Fact

Human bodies contain more microbial genes than human genes—by about 100 to 1. In fact, microbes outnumber our own cells by a factor of at least 1.3 to 1, making us more microbial than human in many ways.

Real-Life Application

The book encourages more mindful antibiotic use, better dietary habits (to support a healthy gut microbiome), and a shift away from unnecessary sterilization. It also inspires deeper respect for the natural world, urging us to protect microbial diversity not just in our bodies, but in ecosystems everywhere.

Recommended Books

  • The Hidden Half of Nature by David R. Montgomery and Anne Biklé – Explores the microbiome’s role in soil and human health.
  • An Elegant Defense by Matt Richtel – Examines the immune system’s complexity, complementing Yong’s insights into microbial interactions.
