Day 144: Move Your DNA by Katy Bowman

Introduction

Is exercise enough to make us healthy? In Move Your DNA, biomechanist and movement educator Katy Bowman challenges conventional fitness thinking by arguing that it's not just how much you move, but how you move—and how often.

Published in 2014, this groundbreaking book proposes that modern diseases—like pelvic floor disorders, osteoporosis, and metabolic issues—aren’t caused by aging or lack of exercise, but by a lack of diverse, natural movement. Just like nutrition, movement is a biological input that shapes your health on a cellular level.

Bowman’s message: Don’t just add exercise into your life. Restructure your life so movement is woven into every moment.

Summary

Bowman redefines what movement means, going beyond exercise to focus on natural, whole-body movement throughout the day. She draws a distinction between “movement” and “exercise,” emphasizing that even those who work out regularly may still be sedentary if they sit for most of the day.

  • Movement is like food: Just as you need a variety of nutrients, your body needs a variety of movements. Repetitive gym routines aren’t enough—our cells need different loads and ranges of motion.
  • Sedentary culture is systemic: Modern life (cars, couches, screens) limits how often and how diversely we move, leading to chronic issues in the feet, hips, spine, and more.
  • Micronutrient movements matter: Small, often-neglected movements like squatting, walking on uneven surfaces, or toe-spreading are crucial for full-body health.
  • Environment shapes movement: The spaces we live and work in affect how much and how well we move. Bowman advocates for minimal footwear, floor sitting, and active furniture.
  • Alignment is key: Poor postural habits contribute to mechanical stress on the body. Restoring alignment and mobility can help relieve pain and dysfunction.

Bowman combines scientific explanation with practical advice, including movement “prescriptions,” alignment tips, and step-by-step guidance to build a life rich in movement.

Core Concept

The central message of Move Your DNA is: Movement is a nutrient. Just like food, it affects us on a cellular level—and deficiencies in certain kinds of movement lead to predictable symptoms and breakdowns.

Fitness doesn’t equal health if movement diversity is missing. To thrive, we need to move more and move better—not just at the gym, but all day long.

Key Insights and Strategies

  • Exercise ≠ Movement: You can be an “active sedentary” person—someone who exercises for an hour but sits for the other 23. Health comes from continuous, varied movement throughout the day.
  • Movement Variety Prevents Wear and Tear: Repetitive motions overload specific tissues, while underusing others. Just like a varied diet, movement diversity supports balanced health.
  • Alignment Affects Cellular Health: The way you hold and use your body affects circulation, nerve function, joint wear, and cellular repair.
  • Shoes Change Your Movement: Modern footwear restricts natural foot mechanics. Going barefoot or using minimal shoes helps restore balance, strength, and sensory input.
  • Movement Environment Matters: Swap chairs for floor cushions, walk on natural terrain, squat instead of sit—your home and workplace can support healthier movement habits.

Quotes to Remember

“You can’t fix the problem of too little movement with just one hour at the gym.”
“Movement is not just about burning calories—it’s about cellular communication.”
“Every movement you make, or don’t make, is shaping your body.”

Practical Application

  • Stand and walk more: make walking your default mode of travel whenever possible.
  • Transition to minimal footwear: strengthen foot muscles and improve posture.
  • Change your sitting habits: sit on the floor, squat, or kneel to engage different muscles.
  • Add movement “snacks”: incorporate calf stretches, toe lifts, shoulder rolls, and squats throughout the day.
  • Rethink your furniture: use standing desks, floor cushions, or active seating that encourages posture shifts.
  • Make movement social and daily: walk with friends, play with kids, do chores mindfully and with intention.

Interesting Facts and Stories

  • You Can Be “Exercised” But Still Sedentary: Bowman uses the term “active sedentary” to describe people who work out daily but spend the rest of their time sitting—and explains why that’s not enough.
  • NASA’s Astronaut Studies Inspired Her Thinking: Research on muscle loss in space revealed that lack of gravity—and thus mechanical loading—quickly breaks down body tissues, mirroring effects of sedentary life on Earth.
  • Your Pelvic Floor Is a Movement Issue: Pelvic health problems, often seen as medical, are reframed as alignment and movement deficiencies related to posture, gait, and sitting habits.
  • She Lives What She Teaches: Bowman and her family live in a movement-rich environment—walking daily, wearing minimal shoes, squatting to rest, and integrating natural movement into everything from work to parenting.
  • Movement Impacts Every System: From circulation to digestion to cognition, Bowman connects diverse forms of movement to biological performance—highlighting how interwoven movement is with health.
