Day 256: Planta Sapiens by Paco Calvo

Introduction

Planta Sapiens: The New Science of Plant Intelligence by Paco Calvo, published in 2022, invites readers to radically rethink what it means to be intelligent—and what it means to be a plant.

Calvo, a professor of philosophy of science and a pioneer in the emerging field of plant neurobiology, makes the provocative case that plants may be sentient, aware, and even intelligent—but in ways that are radically different from animal cognition. Drawing on cutting-edge research, fieldwork, and philosophical reflection, Planta Sapiens offers a new vision of plants not as passive background organisms, but as active, responsive beings with their own form of wisdom.

This is not a book about plants as metaphors. It’s a serious exploration of the question: what if plants think?

The Question at the Root

Calvo begins by challenging the traditional assumption that intelligence requires a brain or nervous system. Plants have neither—but they clearly sense, react, and adapt to their environments in sophisticated ways.

He asks: are we missing something because we define intelligence in a brain-centric, animal-focused way? Could plants possess a kind of vegetal consciousness, distributed through their tissues and systems, invisible to us because we’re looking for the wrong signs?

This question sets the stage for the rest of the book: Can plants be intelligent without being human-like?

What Plants Know

Plants are exquisitely sensitive to their environments. They can:

  • Detect light, gravity, moisture, and chemical signals.
  • Communicate with other plants via airborne volatiles and root exudates.
  • Recognize kin and alter their growth accordingly.
  • “Eavesdrop” on the distress signals of other plants.
  • Form symbiotic relationships with fungi and bacteria.

Calvo shows that plant behavior is not mechanical or robotic—it’s flexible, context-sensitive, and often anticipatory. A plant will grow toward a support structure before touching it. Roots will avoid salty soil before encountering high salinity.

This suggests prediction, decision-making, and perhaps even learning.

Memory, Learning, and Habituation

One of the most striking parts of the book is the evidence that plants can learn from experience.

Calvo describes studies where plants like Mimosa pudica (the sensitive plant) stop closing their leaves when repeatedly dropped—showing signs of habituation, a basic form of learning. They “remember” that the stimulus is not harmful.

Other plants change their growth strategy in response to past conditions, as if adapting based on memory.

Calvo explores whether these behaviors are just chemical reflexes or signs of cognitive plasticity—the plant version of a mind.

A Different Kind of Intelligence

Calvo emphasizes that plant intelligence is non-neural. It doesn’t involve neurons or brains, but it may still involve electrical signaling, chemical messaging, and even networked processing across tissues.

Plants might operate with a kind of distributed intelligence, similar in some ways to slime molds or ant colonies, where behavior emerges from the coordination of decentralized systems.

This challenges our anthropocentric view of cognition. If intelligence can exist without neurons, what else might we be overlooking?

The Language of Plants

Communication is a major theme in the book.

Plants “talk” in complex ways:

  • Chemical signaling to warn neighbors of herbivores.
  • Electrical impulses that transmit information rapidly across the plant body.
  • Root networks that exchange nutrients and information with fungi (the so-called “Wood Wide Web”).

Calvo suggests that if we take this seriously, we must begin to see plants not just as reactive but as informationally active—processing, transmitting, and using information to guide behavior.

Ethics and Plant Sentience

If plants are intelligent in some form, what does that mean for how we treat them?

Calvo doesn’t argue that we should stop eating plants or stop farming. But he does argue that respect for plant life should be rooted not only in ecological concerns but also in recognition of their complex, possibly sentient, existence.

He calls for a new ethics of perception—one that respects the “umwelt,” or subjective experience, of other living beings, even those very unlike us.

Limits and Controversy

Calvo is open about the speculative nature of his work. Plant intelligence is a controversial field, and many scientists are skeptical. There is no consensus that plants are conscious, and Calvo admits we may never know what, if anything, it “feels like” to be a plant.

But he insists that the burden of proof should not lie solely with those who argue plants are aware—but also with those who assume they are not.

The failure to detect cognition may be a failure of imagination, not of reality.

Why This Book Matters

Planta Sapiens is a deeply original and challenging book. It pushes the boundaries of science and philosophy, asking us to reconsider our assumptions about intelligence, consciousness, and what it means to be alive.

Calvo doesn’t just ask whether plants are smart—he asks whether we’re smart enough to understand them.

This book doesn’t offer easy answers, but it offers an important challenge: to look at the living world not just with knowledge, but with curiosity, humility, and openness to other ways of being.

Key Takeaways and Insights

  • Intelligence may not require a brain—plants display behaviors that suggest perception, learning, and decision-making.
  • Plants respond to light, touch, sound, and chemicals with flexible, adaptive strategies.
  • Evidence suggests that plants may be capable of basic learning and memory.
  • Plant communication involves complex chemical and electrical signaling systems.
  • Distributed intelligence—processing information across a network—is a viable model for plant cognition.
  • Our definitions of intelligence and consciousness may be limited by anthropocentric biases.
  • Ethical respect for plants may be based not just on ecology, but on cognition.
  • Wonder, not certainty, is the proper attitude toward complex non-human life.
