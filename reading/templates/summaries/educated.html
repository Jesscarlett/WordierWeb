Day 290: Educated — Tara Westover

Introduction

Educated by Tara Westover is a powerful coming-of-age memoir about the struggle for self-creation, the cost of loyalty to family, and the liberating—but often painful—force of education. Westover, raised in a strict and isolated Mormon survivalist household in rural Idaho, tells the story of growing up without formal schooling, medical care, or contact with mainstream society. Her journey from a childhood defined by fear, violence, and indoctrination to earning a PhD from Cambridge University is one of the most astonishing transformations in modern nonfiction.

The memoir begins on Buck’s Peak, where Tara lives with her parents and six siblings. Her father, Gene, is deeply paranoid and believes that government institutions are part of an evil system designed to brainwash or control people. As a result, Tara is never enrolled in school, rarely taken to a doctor, and taught to distrust the outside world. Her mother, Faye, becomes an herbalist and midwife, providing alternative medicine and assisting with births despite having no formal training.

A Childhood of Isolation and Danger

Childhood in the Westover household is a mix of rugged freedom and constant danger. Tara and her siblings work in their father’s junkyard, performing hazardous tasks without protection. Injuries are common, and even life-threatening accidents are treated at home with herbs rather than medical care. Tara describes her father surviving a massive explosion and her mother suffering brain damage in a car crash, with both refusing treatment in keeping with their beliefs.

As she grows older, Tara faces the increasing violence of her older brother Shawn. What begins as rough play becomes psychological terror and physical abuse. He yanks her by the hair, calls her degrading names, and convinces her she is worthless. Her parents deny or dismiss the abuse, deepening Tara’s confusion and pain.

A Hunger for Learning

Despite her lack of formal education, Tara becomes curious about learning. Inspired by her brother Tyler, who leaves for college, she teaches herself math, grammar, and history. She studies secretly and obsessively, passing the ACT and gaining admission to Brigham Young University. College becomes a turning point—and a shock. Tara arrives with enormous gaps in her knowledge, unaware of basic historical events and academic expectations. But she works relentlessly and her professors recognise her potential.

Breaking Away Through Education

College deepens Tara’s independence and exposes the ideological control of her upbringing. As she confronts her family about Shawn’s abuse, they accuse her of betrayal, claiming she has been corrupted by education. Tara is torn between love and truth. She attempts reconciliation multiple times, only to face denial and gaslighting. The more she learns, the more she recognises the dysfunction she came from.

Tara earns prestigious opportunities to study at Harvard and Cambridge. There, she gains new tools to understand trauma, memory, and abuse. Education becomes not only intellectual liberation but psychological clarity.

Family Conflict and Selfhood

Tara’s relationship with her parents deteriorates when she refuses to stay silent about Shawn’s violence. They frame her independence as sin and accuse her of being influenced by Satan. Extended relatives sever ties under pressure. Tara realises she must choose between her mental health and her family’s expectations. The emotional cost is profound.

Her journey explores how love and loyalty can coexist with harm. Tara wants to believe her parents love her, yet she understands their worldview is incompatible with her survival. Reclaiming her life requires stepping away from them.

The Meaning of Education

As Tara progresses, she reflects on the meaning of education. For her, education is not simply acquiring information but learning to see clearly—to understand herself outside the narratives imposed on her. She writes that you can love someone and still say goodbye. Education becomes the method through which she rewrites her identity.

Conclusion

By the end of the memoir, Tara is estranged from most of her family. She carries both sorrow and clarity. She accepts that her education gave her something her old life never could: the ability to think freely, question authority, and claim her own destiny. Educated is ultimately a story about liberation—intellectual, emotional, and spiritual. Tara Westover’s memoir is a testament to resilience, truth, and the courage required to become oneself.
