Day 282: Playing Big — Tara Mohr

Introduction

Playing Big by Tara Mohr is a thoughtful, transformative guide that helps women recognise and overcome the internal barriers that keep them from stepping into leadership, creativity, and visibility. While external inequalities exist, Mohr argues that much of what holds women back is internal—patterns of thought, self-doubt, and inherited conditioning that shrink their potential. Her goal is not to teach women to become more like traditional leaders, but to help them rise as their authentic, wise, powerful selves.

The book begins with Mohr’s observation that many brilliant, capable women are “playing small” despite talent, ambition, and desire to contribute more meaningfully. These women often feel called to something bigger—creative work, leadership, entrepreneurship, activism—but they hesitate. Mohr breaks down the psychological, cultural, and emotional roots of this hesitation and offers practical tools to overcome it.

Pachad and Yirah: Two Kinds of Fear

One of the most important ideas Mohr introduces is the distinction between two kinds of fear: pachad and yirah, drawn from Hebrew teachings. Pachad is irrational fear—the catastrophising mind, the anxiety-driven voice predicting failure. Yirah is the fear that arises when we are expanding into a bigger version of ourselves; it is awe, energy, spiritual growth in motion. Mohr teaches women to distinguish between these two experiences and to lean into the fear that signals growth rather than shrinking because of the fear that signals danger.

The Inner Critic and the Inner Mentor

Another central concept is the distinction between the Inner Critic and the Inner Mentor. The Inner Critic is harsh, limiting, perfectionistic, anxious, and rooted in childhood survival strategies. It is loud, repetitive, and judgemental. The Inner Mentor, in contrast, is a calm, wise voice that belongs to your older, future self—the woman you will be in ten or twenty years if you live in alignment with your values. Mohr offers exercises to help women access that inner mentor, allowing decisions to be guided by wisdom rather than fear. Learning to recognise and quiet the Inner Critic is a foundational step in playing bigger.

Good Girl Conditioning and Communication Habits

Mohr also addresses the cultural forces that shape women’s behaviour, including the socialisation to seek praise, avoid conflict, and prioritise likability. For generations, women were rewarded for obedience, selflessness, and pleasing others. Without realising it, many carry these patterns into adulthood, shaping the way they speak, write, negotiate, lead, and show up in public spaces. Mohr calls these internalised behaviours “good girl” habits, and she explains how letting go of them is key to stepping into genuine power.

A chapter that resonates with many readers is Mohr’s analysis of how women undermine their own communication through habits such as apologising unnecessarily, using hedging language (like “just,” “kind of,” “maybe”), seeking approval, or waiting for permission. These linguistic patterns have real consequences: they diminish authority, weaken ideas, and reinforce a sense of smallness. Mohr encourages women to speak and write with clarity and confidence, trusting the value of their own voice.

The Trap of Over-Expertise

Another barrier Mohr addresses is the tendency to overvalue expertise. Women often believe they must acquire additional degrees, certifications, or years of experience before they are “ready” to begin. Mohr argues that this is a form of self-doubt and that many transformative contributions come not from experts, but from people willing to offer new ideas, perspectives, and questions. Playing big requires trusting your current wisdom and acting before you feel fully prepared.

Callings and Leaps

A major theme throughout the book is calling—the inner pull toward meaningful work or contribution. Mohr explains that callings often arrive quietly, not boldly, and that they may feel vulnerable or impractical at first. She encourages women to honour these nudges through small, consistent “leaps,” rather than waiting for perfect timing. A leap is a short, bold action that moves an idea from concept into reality. The leap bypasses procrastination, fear, and perfectionism by grounding vision in tangible steps.

Playing Big vs. Playing by Old Rules

Mohr emphasises that playing big is not about ego, ambition, or external recognition—it is about showing up for what you are meant to do. It is about serving others, amplifying your gifts, and responding to the needs of the world. Playing big is not louder, more aggressive leadership; it is embodied, grounded leadership rooted in integrity.

Handling Criticism

The book includes powerful insights about dealing with criticism. Mohr explains that every woman who plays big will be criticised because visibility invites opinions. Instead of fearing criticism, Mohr encourages women to reframe it as feedback about the viewer, not the creator. Criticism reveals more about the critic’s expectations, worldview, or limitations than about the woman being criticised. Learning to interpret criticism wisely allows women to stay aligned with their message and purpose without shrinking.

The Importance of Supportive Communities

Mohr also addresses the importance of supportive communities. Transformation rarely happens alone; women need networks of encouragement, honesty, and shared growth. Mohr encourages women to seek out communities that reflect the person they want to become, not merely the identity they are leaving behind.

Choosing Yourself

One of the book’s most refreshing ideas is that women don’t need to wait to be chosen. Mohr argues that traditional leadership structures rely on gatekeepers—bosses, institutions, reviewers, or evaluators. But in the digital age, women can choose themselves. They can start businesses, publish their ideas, create art, launch projects, and lead movements without waiting for permission. Playing big requires stepping into self-authority.

Conclusion: Becoming Your Fullest Self

Ultimately, Playing Big is about embodying the fullest expression of your gifts. It is about showing up courageously and truthfully, even when it feels uncomfortable. It is about challenging the internal conditioning that says “not yet,” “not good enough,” or “who do you think you are?” Mohr’s message is that the world needs women’s wisdom, creativity, leadership, and voices—and that playing small serves no one.

Mohr’s work invites every woman to listen to her inner mentor, pursue her callings, tolerate the sacred fear of expansion, and trust that she is ready—right now—to play bigger than she ever has before.
