Day 274: Women Who Run With the Wolves by Clarissa Pinkola Estés

Introduction

Women Who Run With the Wolves by Clarissa Pinkola Estés is a powerful blend of mythology, psychology, storytelling, and feminist wisdom. Drawing from her background as a Jungian psychoanalyst and cantadora (keeper of stories), Estés explores the archetype of the “Wild Woman”—the instinctive, intuitive, courageous self that exists within every woman but is often suppressed by culture, fear, and social expectations.

The book uses global folk tales, fairy tales, and myths to illuminate different facets of this Wild Woman archetype. Through these stories, Estés argues that a woman’s vitality, creativity, and emotional truth depend on reconnecting with her natural instincts—her cycles, desires, boundaries, intuition, and fierce knowing.

The Wild Woman Archetype

Estés defines the Wild Woman as the part of the feminine psyche that is deeply connected to instinct, nature, creativity, and freedom. She is not “wild” in a chaotic or destructive sense; rather, she is wild in the sense of being authentic, untamed, and deeply rooted in the truth of who she is.

This aspect of the self is often buried under layers of conditioning, shame, perfectionism, or roles that demand silence, compliance, and emotional restraint. Estés argues that reconnecting with this inner force is essential for a woman’s psychological and spiritual health.

Stories as Maps of the Psyche

Throughout the book, Estés retells ancient stories—such as “Bluebeard,” “La Loba,” “The Handless Maiden,” and “Vasalisa the Wise”—and explains how each reveals patterns of loss, awakening, wounding, and healing within the female psyche. These stories are not entertainment; they are maps, teaching tools, and mirrors.

For instance:

  • “La Loba” (The Wolf Woman) teaches the importance of reclaiming lost parts of the self and singing them back to life.
  • “Bluebeard” warns against predators—internal and external—that seek to silence intuition and destroy creativity.
  • “Vasalisa” highlights the power of inner wisdom and the necessity of listening to one’s “inner doll”—the voice of intuition.
  • “The Handless Maiden” explores trauma, endurance, and the long journey of healing and self-recovery.

Intuition, Instinct, and the Deep Self

One of Estés’ core teachings is that women are born with strong intuition, but cultural systems—family expectations, work pressures, patriarchal norms, and social policing—often train that intuition out of them. The result is a disconnection from instinct, leading to burnout, exhaustion, self-doubt, or feeling lost.

Reconnecting with the Wild Woman means relearning how to listen inwardly, embracing gut feelings as legitimate wisdom, and trusting emotional signals such as anger, longing, or discomfort as guides. Intuition becomes a compass that helps women recognise danger, choose relationships that honour them, and commit to paths that feed their soul.

Cycles of Creativity and Rest

Estés also highlights that creativity moves in natural cycles, just like seasons. There are periods of growth, inspiration, productivity—and equally necessary periods of rest, reflection, and rebuilding. The modern world often demands constant output and punishes rest, but the Wild Woman honours cyclicality.

This recognition allows women to avoid burnout and to value their creative rhythms instead of forcing themselves into relentless productivity. Creativity, Estés argues, is one of the Wild Woman’s essential expressions—it is how she speaks and how she transforms life around her.

Boundaries, Danger, and the Bluebeard Archetype

One of the most famous stories in the book, “Bluebeard,” illustrates the presence of a predatory force—both external (dangerous people) and internal (self-sabotage, doubt, fear)—that seeks to imprison a woman’s vitality. Bluebeard archetypes appear in relationships, workplaces, families, and even inner voices that say “you cannot,” “you are unworthy,” or “stay small.”

By naming and confronting Bluebeard, women reclaim their power, establish boundaries, and refuse to submit to forces that diminish their instincts or silence their truth.

Healing, Recovery, and the Return to Self

Much of Estés’ work revolves around psychological healing. The Wild Woman is often buried after experiences of trauma, criticism, shame, rejection, or emotional neglect. The journey back to her is rarely quick. It involves rest, rituals, self-compassion, therapy, solitude, nature, and deep inner work.

Healing is portrayed not as a linear process but as a spiral, with each cycle bringing greater clarity, strength, and self-understanding. Each story in the book offers a layer of insight into how women can reclaim lost instincts, rebuild confidence, and embrace their full emotional range.

Relationships and the Wild Woman

In relationships, the Wild Woman values authenticity and mutual respect. She seeks partners who honour her independence, support her creativity, and allow her to express her desires and boundaries openly. Estés argues that many relationship struggles arise when women deny their instincts to “keep the peace,” which leads to resentment and loss of self.

Healthy relationships, she suggests, require both parties to show up as whole, honest, evolving individuals—not as confined roles or expectations.

The Call to Rewild the Self

Ultimately, Women Who Run With the Wolves is an invitation to come home to one’s deepest, most instinctive self. It urges women to reclaim their voice, their creativity, their sensuality, their cycles, their intuition, and their courage. Rewilding does not mean recklessness; it means truthfulness—living in alignment with what the soul knows.

Estés offers a reminder that every woman carries an inner wolf: wise, fierce, protective, curious, playful, and powerful. When that inner wolf is freed, a woman not only transforms her own life but also strengthens her ability to nurture, create, protect, and inspire those around her.

Why This Book Matters

Women Who Run With the Wolves is celebrated for its depth, beauty, and transformative psychological insight. It validates experiences many women feel but have never been able to articulate. Through myth and story, Estés provides a language for inner struggle and a roadmap for reclaiming strength, intuition, and freedom.

This book matters because it reminds women that their instincts are sacred, their voices are necessary, and their lives are meant to be lived fully—not constrained, diminished, or silenced. It is a guide back to the ancient, untamed wisdom that has always lived within.
