Day 284: Year of Yes — Shonda Rhimes

Introduction

Year of Yes by Shonda Rhimes is an empowering, funny, deeply honest memoir about what happens when one of the busiest, most successful women in Hollywood decides to say “yes” to the things that scare her. Rhimes, the powerhouse creator behind Grey’s Anatomy, Scandal, and How to Get Away with Murder, reveals that despite her career success, she had been living a life shaped by fear, avoidance, overwork, and emotional withdrawal. When her sister casually says, “You never say yes to anything,” Rhimes realises she has been shrinking from experiences that could bring joy, growth, and connection. So she makes a decision: for one entire year, she will say yes to whatever frightens or challenges her.

The book is structured around the themes and lessons Rhimes learns through this year-long experiment. What begins as a simple commitment turns into a profound transformation that reshapes her relationships, career, self-worth, and understanding of happiness.

Facing Fear Through Public Speaking

One of the first fears she confronts is public speaking. Despite her fame, Rhimes avoided interviews, panels, and appearances. Saying yes forces her to confront anxiety and self-doubt. In one pivotal moment, she agrees to give the commencement speech at Dartmouth, her alma mater. The speech becomes a viral sensation, but more importantly, it becomes a moment where she recognises her own strength. Rhimes learns that fear shrinks when courage grows, and courage grows through practice—not perfection.

Embracing Opportunities

Another major theme is embracing opportunities instead of hiding from them. Rhimes admits she was trapped in a cycle of saying no out of habit, not intention. Invitations to events, social gatherings, collaborations, and personal moments—she declined them all. In saying yes, she reconnects with friends, meets new people, and discovers that joy often lies on the other side of discomfort.

Saying Yes to Her Body

One of the most emotional sections explores saying yes to her own body. Rhimes confronts her complicated relationship with weight, health, and identity. She doesn’t frame this as a diet story, but as a story about reclaiming ownership of her physical wellbeing. Saying yes means listening to her body, valuing it, and treating it with care rather than shame. This leads to unexpected transformation—both physical and emotional.

Saying Yes to Play and Joy

A significant part of Rhimes’s journey involves saying yes to play. As a high-achieving workaholic, she had lost touch with spontaneity, joy, and fun. She writes passionately about rediscovering playfulness through her children. Her daughters remind her that imagination, laughter, and silliness are not luxuries—they are essential to aliveness. Saying yes means getting on the floor, dancing in the kitchen, joining in games, and choosing presence over productivity.

Authenticity and Emotional Openness

The book also explores saying yes to authenticity. Rhimes acknowledges that, for years, she hid behind her professional persona—powerful, accomplished, always in control. But inside, she felt insecure, lonely, and disconnected. Saying yes means allowing herself to be seen fully, including the vulnerable parts. She begins having honest conversations, setting boundaries, and acknowledging her emotional needs. This leads to significant personal growth and deeper relationships.

Saying Yes to Saying No

One of the most powerful lessons Rhimes learns is saying yes to saying no. She realises that “yes” doesn’t mean pleasing everyone or taking on more work. Sometimes the bravest form of yes is saying no to requests, expectations, and obligations that drain her energy or compromise her wellbeing. She stops overworking, stops taking on every project, and begins prioritising rest, presence, and joy. Rhimes argues that women, especially, are taught to be endlessly accommodating, and that reclaiming “no” is essential for empowerment.

Owning Her Success

Rhimes also says yes to her own voice. For years, she downplayed her accomplishments or dismissed her talent. She felt uncomfortable with praise and hesitant to take credit. Learning to own her success becomes a crucial part of her transformation. She stops undermining herself and begins celebrating her achievements with confidence rather than apology.

Re-evaluating Relationships

Her journey also includes saying yes to hard truths—especially about friendship. She realises that some relationships in her life were one-sided or draining. Saying yes to growth means releasing these connections with honesty. Rhimes refuses to maintain friendships that disrespect her boundaries or fail to reciprocate care. She chooses relationships rooted in mutual respect, trust, and support.

Saying Yes to Nontraditional Choices

A surprising revelation in the book is Rhimes’s decision to say yes to not wanting marriage. She reflects on societal expectations and the pressures placed on women to follow traditional paths. Rhimes acknowledges that she does not desire marriage, and instead embraces a life shaped by her own values—not external judgments. This acceptance becomes one of the most liberating yeses of her year.

Internal Transformation

Another notable theme is the idea that success is not a substitute for self-love. Rhimes had achieved extraordinary professional milestones, but she still struggled to feel worthy, lovable, or joyful. Saying yes reconnects her with gratitude, self-respect, and presence. She learns that internal transformation matters as much as external achievement.

Conclusion

Ultimately, Year of Yes is a rallying cry to stop living small. It is a reminder that bravery is built one yes at a time, that fear fades when faced regularly, and that meaningful change is possible when we step outside our comfort zones. Rhimes shows that saying yes is not about recklessness or overcommitment—but about choosing aliveness, authenticity, and joy. Her message is clear: Life expands or contracts in proportion to your courage. Say yes to the life you want. Say yes to becoming the person you are meant to be.
