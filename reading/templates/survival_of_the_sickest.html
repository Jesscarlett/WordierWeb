Day 263: Survival of the Sickest by Dr. Sharon Moalem

Introduction

Survival of the Sickest: A Medical Maverick Discovers Why We Need Disease by Dr. Sharon Moalem, published in 2007, presents a provocative and fascinating thesis: many of the diseases and genetic disorders we consider harmful today may have evolved because they once helped humans survive.

By weaving together evolutionary biology, medicine, and genetics, Moalem reframes disease not as a failure of evolution—but as a consequence of its success.

This is a book about paradoxes: why conditions like hemochromatosis, diabetes, high cholesterol, and even anemia might once have been advantages in certain environments. In a world where medicine often treats symptoms, Moalem steps back and asks: what if those symptoms were once solutions?

The Central Idea: Evolution Favors Survival, Not Perfection

Moalem’s core message is that natural selection doesn’t care about long-term health, comfort, or longevity. It only “cares” about what increases the odds of reproduction in a given environment.

Traits that are dangerous in today’s world may have been crucial in the past. In other words: being genetically predisposed to a disease might once have saved your ancestors’ lives.

He calls this the “survival of the sickest”—the idea that some diseases survived because they conferred survival benefits under certain environmental pressures.

Hemochromatosis and the Black Death

One of the most compelling examples in the book is hemochromatosis, a genetic disorder in which the body absorbs too much iron. Left untreated, it can lead to liver failure, heart disease, and diabetes.

But Moalem suggests that during the Black Death, hemochromatosis might have been a protective trait. Bacteria thrive on iron, and people with hemochromatosis store iron in tissues, not in blood plasma—making it less accessible to pathogens.

This means those with the disorder may have been more likely to survive the plague, passing the gene on.

Diabetes and the Ice Age

Moalem explores the “thrifty gene” hypothesis and suggests that diabetes may have conferred a survival benefit in cold climates.

High blood sugar can act as a natural antifreeze, preventing cell damage from extreme cold. In Ice Age Europe, a mild form of diabetes might have helped people survive freezing temperatures by protecting their tissues.

Today, with an abundance of food and no Ice Age in sight, that same trait contributes to metabolic disease.

Cholesterol, Heart Disease, and Parasites

Another paradox Moalem investigates is cholesterol. While we associate high cholesterol with heart disease, Moalem notes that cholesterol plays a critical role in immune defense.

Cholesterol-rich membranes are harder for viruses to penetrate. In an era of rampant infections and parasites, higher cholesterol may have helped defend against deadly pathogens.

In modern times—with antibiotics, clean water, and vaccines—the balance has shifted, and the once-helpful trait becomes a risk factor.

Anemia and Malaria

Moalem also discusses iron-deficiency anemia, one of the most common nutritional problems worldwide.

But in malaria-endemic regions, anemia may be protective. Like with hemochromatosis, limiting iron availability makes it harder for parasites like Plasmodium (the cause of malaria) to multiply in the bloodstream.

In this way, a “deficiency” might actually be an evolutionary strategy.

G6PD Deficiency and Genetic Trade-Offs

Survival of the Sickest also explores G6PD deficiency, a genetic trait that can cause red blood cells to break down under stress (like after eating fava beans or taking certain medications).

But in the context of malaria, this trait disrupts the parasite’s ability to thrive—again showing that what we consider a disorder today may have been an advantage in the past.

This leads to one of the book’s main themes: genetic trade-offs. A single gene can have both positive and negative effects depending on the environment.

Epigenetics and Inherited Experience

In later chapters, Moalem explores epigenetics—how environmental factors can change gene expression without altering DNA.

  • How famine, stress, or toxins can affect not only an individual’s health, but that of their descendants.
  • How gene expression patterns are influenced by nutrition, behavior, and exposure to pathogens.

Epigenetics supports Moalem’s broader argument: biology is not fixed. Evolution is not just about DNA—it’s also about how genes respond to changing environments.

Why This Book Matters

Survival of the Sickest is a bold and accessible book that asks us to rethink what disease means. Rather than viewing illness solely as a flaw, Moalem asks us to consider its evolutionary context.

By highlighting the adaptive benefits of harmful traits, he adds depth to our understanding of genetics, public health, and human survival.

The book encourages a holistic and historical perspective on medicine—one that honors complexity and questions conventional wisdom.

Key Takeaways and Insights

  • Many genetic disorders may have evolved as survival strategies in specific environments.
  • Traits like hemochromatosis, diabetes, and anemia may have conferred resistance to infections or cold.
  • Evolution selects for reproductive success—not long-term health.
  • The same gene can be beneficial or harmful depending on context.
  • Modern environments have changed the cost-benefit analysis of ancient adaptations.
  • Cholesterol, iron, and blood sugar serve vital functions beyond their modern risk associations.
  • Epigenetics shows that environmental effects can be passed down generations.
  • Understanding the evolutionary roots of disease can lead to more nuanced and effective medical thinking.
