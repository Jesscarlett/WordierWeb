Day 254: The Bird Way by Jennifer Ackerman

Introduction

The Bird Way by Jennifer Ackerman, published in 2020, is a follow-up to her acclaimed book The Genius of Birds. This time, Ackerman broadens the scope—traveling deeper into the diversity of global bird life to show that there is no single way to be a bird.

With fresh field research, global perspectives, and vivid storytelling, Ackerman reveals that birds don’t just mimic—they innovate. They don’t just follow instinct—they strategize. And far from being rule-bound creatures of habit, birds live in complex, dynamic, and highly varied worlds.

Rethinking the “Standard Bird”

Ackerman opens by challenging the outdated idea of a “default” bird—a European or North American songbird that sets the standard for avian behavior. In reality, most bird species live outside temperate zones, and their behaviors often differ dramatically from what Western science once assumed was “normal.”

Birds around the world do things we once thought only humans could do: solve problems, teach each other, cooperate in groups, deceive enemies, and even express individuality.

How Birds Talk

Ackerman explores the complexity of bird communication, diving into songs, calls, mimicry, and even deception.

  • The Australian superb lyrebird, capable of mimicking chainsaws, camera shutters, and other birds.
  • Drongos of the Kalahari, which mimic alarm calls of other species to scare animals away from food—and then swoop in to steal it.

Birds communicate more than just warnings or mating calls. They share information, express emotions, and engage in social manipulation—proving that bird language is far richer than we once imagined.

How Birds Work

Ackerman breaks down avian labor—how birds forage, hunt, build, and problem-solve.

  • Palm cockatoos use sticks to drum on trees as communication.
  • Egyptian vultures crack open ostrich eggs with rocks.
  • Acorn woodpeckers raise chicks cooperatively with help from family members.

How Birds Play

Birds:

  • Roll down hills and slide on snow.
  • Play aerial games.
  • Engage in object manipulation and social teasing.

Play is not just fun—it’s practice, learning, and creativity. Birds use it to hone their skills, build social bonds, and sometimes just enjoy life.

How Birds Parent

Ackerman explores:

  • Jacanas, where males raise chicks while females mate with multiple partners.
  • Fairy-wrens that teach unborn chicks special “passwords.”
  • Cuckoos that lay eggs in other birds’ nests and manipulate them into parenting.

How Birds Think

Ackerman dives into:

  • Parrot intelligence—memory, imitation, and tool use.
  • Corvids—planning, geometry, cause-and-effect reasoning.

Birds evaluate, learn, and remember. Their cognition may be different from ours, but it is just as valid and impressive.

Female Birds: The Hidden Half

Ackerman highlights:

  • Females with complex songs and territorial roles.
  • Misinterpretations caused by male-centered assumptions in science.

Correcting these gender biases not only adds fairness—it improves scientific understanding.

The Global Bird Perspective

From Papua New Guinea to the Amazon, Ackerman draws from scientists around the world—many of them women—and incorporates Indigenous knowledge. This global lens breaks old assumptions and widens our view of bird behavior.

Why This Book Matters

The Bird Way is a stunning re-examination of what it means to be intelligent, creative, emotional, and social—if you happen to have wings. It broadens our view of the natural world and invites humility, curiosity, and awe.

Key Takeaways and Insights

  • There is no “standard” bird—avian behavior is incredibly diverse.
  • Birds communicate using mimicry, manipulation, and shared learning.
  • Many species use tools, cooperate, and innovate new strategies.
  • Play in birds is linked to creativity and skill-building.
  • Parenting in birds is as varied and strategic as in humans.
  • Female birds have been overlooked—understanding them completes the picture.
  • Bird intelligence may be different, but it is equally valid and powerful.
  • Science must continue to expand its lens, including global and cultural perspectives.
