Day 132: The Body Keeps the Score by Bessel van der Kolk

Introduction

Trauma doesn’t just live in the mind—it embeds itself in the body. In The Body Keeps the Score: Brain, Mind, and Body in the Healing of Trauma, psychiatrist Bessel van der Kolk presents decades of clinical experience and neuroscience research to show how trauma reshapes both our brain circuits and bodily systems. Published in 2014, the book is a powerful combination of science, case studies, and healing strategies aimed at helping people understand and recover from trauma.

Van der Kolk is a pioneer in trauma studies, having worked with war veterans, abuse survivors, and patients suffering from PTSD for over four decades. His key message is that healing requires more than just talking—it requires reconnecting with the body, regaining a sense of safety, and re-establishing control over our physiological responses.

Summary

The book unfolds in five parts:

  • The Rediscovery of Trauma
  • This is Your Brain on Trauma
  • The Minds of Children
  • The Imprint of Trauma
  • Paths to Recovery

Van der Kolk starts by mapping the history of trauma research and how it was marginalized by mainstream psychiatry until the late 20th century. He discusses how trauma isn’t only about the event itself—it’s about how our body and nervous system respond to that event.

In Part Two, he explores the neurological basis of trauma. Traumatic experiences can dysregulate the amygdala, hippocampus, and prefrontal cortex—regions tied to fear, memory, and rational thinking. Trauma survivors often live in a heightened state of vigilance, unable to distinguish between real and perceived threats.

Part Three addresses childhood trauma, particularly attachment injuries and abuse. These early traumas impact brain development, stress hormones, and emotional regulation, leaving long-lasting imprints. Van der Kolk argues that childhood trauma is often misdiagnosed as ADHD or behavioral disorders.

In Part Four, he explains how trauma lives in the body, manifesting as chronic tension, digestive issues, autoimmune responses, and sleep disturbances. He emphasizes that talking about trauma can sometimes re-trigger the response rather than resolve it.

Finally, Part Five explores paths to healing—many of which lie outside traditional talk therapy. These include yoga, EMDR (Eye Movement Desensitization and Reprocessing), neurofeedback, Internal Family Systems therapy, and theatre and movement-based therapies. Healing, he concludes, involves helping the body learn that the trauma is over.

Core Concept

The central idea is simple but radical: the body keeps a visceral, cellular memory of trauma. The brain alone cannot resolve trauma, because trauma is not just a cognitive wound—it's somatic. Unless the body is brought into healing, the symptoms persist.

Van der Kolk highlights that trauma is not about the story of what happened. It’s about the overwhelming, disempowering sensations it left behind in the body—tight chests, racing hearts, frozen limbs. The healing journey, therefore, must involve reintegrating the body, restoring self-regulation, and rebuilding safe human connection.

Key Insights and Strategies

  • Trauma Is a Body Experience: Traumatized individuals often become disconnected from their physical sensations. Healing starts by restoring awareness and trust in the body through practices like yoga and somatic therapy.
  • The Brain Is Rewired by Trauma: Trauma alters brain functions responsible for safety, memory, and emotion. This is why trauma survivors are often stuck in fight-or-flight mode, even in safe environments.
  • Talk Therapy Has Its Limits: While talking can help, verbal processing isn’t always enough. Therapies that bypass language—like EMDR or neurofeedback—can help access and reset trauma-encoded brain patterns.
  • Safe Relationships Are Crucial: Human connection is essential for recovery. Group therapy, supportive communities, and empathetic caregivers help rebuild broken trust and foster resilience.
  • Movement and Creativity Aid Healing: Activities like theatre, dance, and art help trauma survivors express unspoken pain and reclaim agency over their narratives and bodies.

Quotes to Remember

“The body keeps the score: if the memory of trauma is encoded in the viscera, in heartbreaking and gut-wrenching emotions, then the cure must involve the body.”
“Being able to feel safe with other people is probably the single most important aspect of mental health.”
“Trauma results in a fundamental reorganization of the way the mind and brain manage perceptions.”

Practical Application

  • Develop body awareness through gentle practices like yoga, breathing exercises, or tai chi.
  • Work with therapists trained in trauma modalities like EMDR, somatic experiencing, or neurofeedback.
  • Create safe spaces—physically and emotionally—where you can feel secure and not judged.
  • Acknowledge the body’s signals instead of overriding them. Learn to listen to tension, fatigue, or discomfort as cues.
  • Engage in creative outlets that allow non-verbal expression—art, music, dance, or journaling.

For caregivers, educators, and clinicians, this book calls for trauma-informed environments that prioritize safety, choice, and connection. Trauma is not rare—it’s a public health issue. Healing must go beyond diagnosis and symptom control.

Impact and Reception

The Body Keeps the Score has become a bible of trauma healing. It’s been on bestseller lists for years and has influenced therapists, educators, policy makers, and survivors worldwide. The book has shifted how the public and professionals understand trauma—not just as a psychological disorder, but as a whole-body response that demands holistic healing.

Its impact is visible in the growing popularity of somatic therapies, trauma-informed teaching, and integrative mental health practices. The book also sparked new conversations about childhood adversity, PTSD, and the need for compassionate, embodied care systems.
