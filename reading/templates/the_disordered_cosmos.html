Day 115: The Disordered Cosmos by Chanda Prescod-Weinstein

Introduction

Chanda Prescod-Weinstein’s The Disordered Cosmos: A Journey into Dark Matter, Spacetime, and Dreams Deferred is an extraordinary blend of science, social justice, and personal memoir. Published in 2021, this book reimagines what it means to engage with physics—not just as a discipline of discovery but as a space where power, race, gender, and identity converge. Prescod-Weinstein is a theoretical physicist and one of the first Black women in the U.S. to earn a PhD in the field. Her voice offers a fresh and necessary perspective on both the cosmos and the culture of science itself.

What sets this book apart is its multidimensional approach: while deeply rooted in physics, it equally centers justice, inclusion, and lived experience. It challenges readers to see scientific inquiry as inherently political and to interrogate who gets to do science, under what conditions, and for whose benefit. This is not just a book about quarks and dark matter—it’s about dismantling oppressive systems while keeping our eyes on the stars.

Summary

The Disordered Cosmos weaves together science, autobiography, and critique of the scientific establishment across four broad themes.

  • The Beauty of Physics – Prescod-Weinstein explores foundational topics in particle physics and cosmology, including the Standard Model, quantum field theory, and the elusive nature of dark matter. She shares her passion for the elegant structures of the universe.
  • The Structure of Inequality – She scrutinizes the exclusionary nature of academia and physics, especially for Black, Indigenous, queer, and disabled people. She recounts microaggressions, systemic bias, and gatekeeping in scientific institutions.
  • Language and Power – Prescod-Weinstein discusses how scientific language and naming conventions reflect colonial and patriarchal assumptions, such as the term “dark matter” and how we define objectivity.
  • Dreams and Futures – The book closes with a call for a just future in science—one where curiosity, accessibility, and equity are as fundamental as the speed of light.

Prescod-Weinstein invites readers to love the cosmos and work to make the world better for those who study it.

Memorable Insight or Case Study

A powerful moment in the book is her critique of the term “dark matter.” She reflects on how naming it “dark” can unconsciously reinforce racial associations and marginalization. While not suggesting we rename it, she uses this example to illuminate how language shapes perception—even in supposedly objective fields like physics.

Key Study Points

  • Science is Not Culturally Neutral – It’s shaped by social hierarchies and exclusionary practices.
  • Physics is Beautiful and Political – The awe of discovery coexists with the need for equity.
  • Naming and Framing Matter – Even scientific terms carry historical and social implications.
  • Representation is Vital – Diverse voices in science lead to richer, more ethical inquiry.
  • We Must Reimagine the Scientific Community – Equity and justice are as important as equations.

Key Concepts and Explanation

  • The Standard Model of Particle Physics – Describes the fundamental particles and forces (except gravity).
    Relevance: Prescod-Weinstein uses it to explore both physical laws and systemic barriers in learning them.
  • Dark Matter – A mysterious substance making up ~85% of the universe’s matter, detectable only through gravitational effects.
    Analogy: Like a ghost in the gravitational fabric—known only by its absence.
  • Decolonizing Science – A movement to question and dismantle Eurocentric assumptions in scientific disciplines.
    Example: Recognizing Indigenous contributions to astronomy and rejecting objectivity as the sole marker of truth.
  • Science as a Human Endeavor – She emphasizes that science is practiced by people with histories, identities, and biases.
    Implication: Accountability and care should guide scientific culture.

Quotes

“I am a theoretical physicist, and I am a Black woman. I am not an oxymoron.”
“Science is not always the pursuit of truth; sometimes it is the pursuit of power.”

Fun or Surprising Fact

Prescod-Weinstein explains that axions, a leading dark matter candidate, were originally theoretical fixes for an unsolved problem in quantum chromodynamics—not invented to explain dark matter at all. Their journey from mathematical artifact to cosmic mystery illustrates the strange beauty of theoretical physics.

Real-Life Application

This book urges readers to challenge injustice not just in society, but within science itself. Supporting inclusive education, mentoring underrepresented students, and advocating for equity in STEM are ways readers can help build a more just scientific world—while also learning to love the stars.

Recommended Books

  • Inferior by Angela Saini – Investigates how gender bias has shaped science, echoing Prescod-Weinstein’s critiques.
  • The End of Everything (Astrophysically Speaking) by Katie Mack – A lively, accessible tour of cosmic endings, written by another brilliant physicist with a fresh voice.
