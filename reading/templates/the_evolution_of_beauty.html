Day 252: The Evolution of Beauty by Richard O. Prum

Introduction

The Evolution of Beauty by Richard O. Prum, published in 2017, challenges conventional views on evolution by reintroducing an idea that Charles Darwin proposed but that many biologists later sidelined: aesthetic mate choice.

Prum, an ornithologist and evolutionary biologist, argues that beauty—particularly as perceived through the eyes and preferences of female animals—has been a major driver of evolution, independent of survival benefits. This idea reframes natural selection not just as a mechanism of brute survival, but also one of freedom, expression, and desire.

The Forgotten Half of Darwin’s Theory

Charles Darwin famously proposed natural selection as the main force of evolution, where traits that improve survival and reproduction are passed on. But in The Descent of Man, Darwin also introduced sexual selection—especially mate choice—as a second evolutionary engine.

Darwin believed that some traits evolve not because they help an organism survive, but because they are attractive to potential mates. Peacocks’ tails, birdsong, and vibrant plumage, Darwin argued, are examples of beauty shaped by aesthetic preference, especially from females.

Over time, however, most scientists came to favor the “good genes” hypothesis—arguing that beauty signals health or genetic fitness, rather than preference for beauty’s sake. Prum disputes this, siding with Darwin’s original idea: sometimes beauty evolves because it is beautiful—full stop.

Beauty as a Force in Evolution

Prum provides examples from his lifelong study of birds, particularly birds of paradise, manakins, and bowerbirds. These species have evolved elaborate feathers, dances, and displays that seem to serve no survival function—and in many cases, make survival harder.

According to Prum, the answer lies in female choice. Over generations, female preferences for certain aesthetic traits shape the direction of evolution, even at the cost of survival advantage.

This idea—called aesthetic evolution—suggests that beauty itself is a legitimate evolutionary force, one driven by subjective desire, not survival efficiency.

The Agency of Female Animals

A central theme in Prum’s work is the agency of females in shaping evolution. In rejecting theories that treat female choice as a proxy for health evaluation, he argues instead that female animals have real, active preferences—and that these preferences create evolutionary feedback loops.

For example, if female manakins prefer males with certain dance routines, then males evolve better dances, and females continue to refine their preferences. This loop creates increasingly extravagant behavior and appearance, independent of survival pressure.

This stands in contrast to the idea that beauty always signals better genes or health. Sometimes, traits evolve simply because females like them. Preference becomes its own evolutionary driver.

Beauty, Biology, and Freedom

Prum pushes this idea further: beauty is an expression of freedom. When female animals choose based on aesthetic pleasure, they are expressing autonomy, disrupting male dominance, and shaping the direction of evolution through desire.

This idea has far-reaching implications—not just for birds, but for how we understand sexuality, gender, and human evolution.

Prum provocatively suggests that human traits like artistic expression, body ornamentation, and even sexual diversity may also be the result of aesthetic evolution—not just survival fitness.

Human Evolution and Aesthetic Choice

In the later chapters, Prum applies his theory to humans. He discusses:

  • The evolution of facial symmetry, dance, and song.
  • Human attraction to body art, makeup, and clothing.
  • The role of female choice in shaping reduced male aggression over time.
  • The emergence of same-sex behavior as a natural part of sexual expression, not as a side effect or anomaly.

He argues that many aspects of human culture—especially those related to sexuality and beauty—are products of aesthetic evolution. This theory challenges the view that all traits must contribute to survival or reproduction in obvious ways.

Critique of Mainstream Evolutionary Biology

Prum doesn’t shy away from critiquing mainstream evolutionary thought. He argues that evolutionary biology has often ignored or distorted Darwin’s sexual selection theory, downplaying the role of female animals in shaping evolutionary outcomes.

He accuses some scientists of smuggling conservative or patriarchal assumptions into their models—treating female animals as passive or as mere filters for male fitness, rather than as creative agents of change.

Prum’s feminist reading of evolution is both bold and controversial—but rooted in data and observation.

Why This Book Matters

The Evolution of Beauty challenges readers to rethink how evolution works. It re-centers the role of aesthetics, choice, and female agency in shaping the diversity of life on Earth.

By taking Darwin’s forgotten theory seriously, Prum expands our understanding of what evolution can be. He blends rigorous science with philosophical inquiry, producing a deeply original and thought-provoking book.

This is not just a book about birds or beauty—it’s about what it means to be alive, to choose, and to create.

Key Takeaways and Insights

  • Darwin proposed both natural selection and sexual selection, but the latter has been neglected or misunderstood.
  • Aesthetic mate choice means traits can evolve purely because they are preferred—not because they signal survival or fitness.
  • Female animals have real agency in evolution and can shape species through desire.
  • Many flamboyant traits in birds—like long tails, complex songs, or courtship dances—evolved through preference, not practicality.
  • Beauty is a form of freedom and creativity, not just a reproductive tool.
  • Human behavior, sexuality, and culture may also reflect aesthetic evolution.
  • The mainstream “good genes” theory may reflect unconscious bias rather than biological truth.
  • Evolution is not always efficient—it can be whimsical, extravagant, and delightfully unpredictable.
