Day 213: The Inner Life of Animals by Peter Wohlleben

Introduction

Do animals feel joy? Do they mourn their dead, nurture friendships, or even possess a sense of justice?

In The Inner Life of Animals, German forester and nature writer Peter Wohlleben invites us to reconsider what we think we know about animal behavior and emotion. Drawing on scientific studies, observations from his forestry career, and a deep sense of empathy, Wohlleben makes a compelling case that animals are far more sentient, emotional, and complex than we give them credit for.

This book isn’t just a gentle celebration of the animal world—it’s a call to rethink our relationship with creatures we share the planet with.

Beyond Instinct: Animals as Emotional Beings

Wohlleben’s central thesis is simple, yet radical: animals have inner lives.

He recounts stories that challenge the mechanistic view of animal behavior as mere instinct. For instance, a wild deer that seems to feel embarrassment when caught in an awkward situation. Or a sow that mourns her piglets for days, refusing to eat.

Scientific research backs up much of what Wohlleben observes. Crows that recognize human faces and hold grudges. Mice that exhibit empathy and help distressed cage mates. Chickens that teach their young, and pigs that learn by watching one another.

These are not mindless creatures following hardwired patterns. They are thinking, feeling beings, capable of decision-making and emotional nuance.

Grief, Love, and Loyalty

Some of the most moving chapters explore how animals experience grief and connection.

Wohlleben describes magpies that appear to hold funerals—gathering around the body of a fallen companion and placing blades of grass beside it. He recalls a female deer that repeatedly returns to a spot where her fawn died, long after the area has been cleared.

He shares stories of animals forming lifelong bonds—like a pair of geese that refuse to leave each other’s sides, even in danger. Or a crow that brings gifts to a human who feeds it, demonstrating gratitude and reciprocity.

In many ways, these emotional bonds mirror those we see in humans. And they challenge the idea that animals are incapable of deeper feeling.

Parenting and Teaching

Wohlleben highlights the remarkable ways animals raise their young.

Many species engage in behaviors that go beyond mere reproduction. Red deer teach their fawns where to feed safely. Wild boars discipline their piglets gently but firmly. Ravens guide their fledglings to safe nesting grounds.

In some species, both parents share child-rearing duties. In others, siblings help protect and nurture younger offspring.

What’s remarkable is the intentionality behind these actions. Parents adjust their responses based on the situation, showing flexibility and problem-solving skills. This suggests a level of awareness and emotional intelligence often reserved only for humans or primates.

The Moral Lives of Animals

Do animals have a sense of fairness? Can they make ethical decisions?

Wohlleben explores this idea by sharing examples of animals responding to perceived injustice. Monkeys refuse to cooperate if they see others getting better rewards. Dogs sulk or protest when treated unfairly.

In social animals like wolves or elephants, group harmony depends on rules of conduct. Dominant individuals may intervene to stop fights, share food, or comfort those in distress. These behaviors point to social intelligence—and perhaps even a rudimentary moral code.

While we can’t say animals understand “right” and “wrong” in human terms, they clearly recognize fairness, empathy, and loyalty in their own ways.

Humans, Animals, and the Ethics of Coexistence

Wohlleben argues that we need to rethink how we treat animals—not just wild ones, but domestic and farm animals too.

He criticizes industrial farming practices that ignore animal suffering. Pigs kept in cramped spaces, chickens denied access to sunlight, and calves separated from mothers all violate the emotional well-being of creatures that feel deeply.

He also challenges assumptions that humans are “above” animals because of language, technology, or abstract reasoning. Instead, he encourages us to see ourselves as fellow travelers in the great web of life—differently gifted, but equally deserving of respect.

Quotes to Remember

“Animals feel pleasure and suffer pain—just like us.”
“The line between animal and human is not as firm as we once believed.”
“To understand animals, we must stop seeing them as machines and start seeing them as minds.”

Why This Book Matters

The Inner Life of Animals is not a biology textbook or a romanticized tale. It is a thoughtful, compassionate inquiry into the hidden emotional world of animals.

Wohlleben’s style is warm, conversational, and personal. He mixes science with storytelling, facts with feeling. He doesn’t pretend to have all the answers—but he does ask the right questions.

He invites us to look closer. To see the intelligence in a squirrel caching nuts, the affection in a dog’s eyes, the heartbreak in a mother fox who loses her cubs.

Most importantly, he reminds us that we are not alone in feeling.

Other species love. They grieve. They teach. They remember. They hope. And the more we learn about them, the more we realize we have a responsibility—not just to protect their habitats, but to honor their lives.

In a world that often forgets to stop and observe, The Inner Life of Animals is a quiet, powerful reminder that every creature has a story—and a soul.
