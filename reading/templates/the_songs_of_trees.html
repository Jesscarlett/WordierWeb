Day 220: The Songs of Trees by David George Haskell

Introduction

What if trees could tell us their stories?

In The Songs of Trees, biologist and writer David George Haskell takes us around the world to explore the lives of twelve remarkable trees. Through their branches, roots, and the networks they form, Haskell uncovers a deep truth: trees are not solitary beings. They are connected—to other trees, to animals, to fungi, to humans, and to the living fabric of the Earth.

Published in 2017, The Songs of Trees blends science, travel, and lyrical storytelling to reveal that trees “sing” through their relationships. By listening to these songs, we can better understand the intricate web of life and our place within it.

A Global Journey Through Trees

Haskell’s journey spans continents and ecosystems. Each chapter centers on a different tree in a specific place:

  • A ceibo tree in the Amazon rainforest
  • An olive tree in Jerusalem
  • A maple in an urban park in Tennessee
  • A pear tree in Manhattan
  • A redwood in California
  • A baobab in Africa
  • And others across Japan, Scotland, and the Middle East

Through these diverse locations, Haskell reveals the unique and universal ways trees live, communicate, and connect.

Each tree is a gateway to stories of geology, climate, history, and culture. The book is both intimate and expansive—a celebration of local details and global patterns.

The Song is Connection

At the heart of the book is the idea that trees don’t stand alone.

They form partnerships with fungi, exchanging nutrients through underground networks. They shelter insects, birds, and mammals. They respond to wind, water, and even the sounds of the forest.

In urban environments, trees connect to human communities, shaping our histories and providing shelter, beauty, and resilience.

The “songs” Haskell refers to are not literal melodies, but the symphony of interactions—a continuous exchange of signals, sounds, scents, and growth.

Each tree’s song is the story of its relationships.

Science Meets Storytelling

Haskell draws on the latest ecological research, but he also leans into storytelling, weaving cultural history, personal anecdotes, and philosophical reflection.

He listens not just with instruments, but with attention—with a desire to hear what we usually overlook.

Whether he’s climbing a redwood to observe its canopy, tracing the history of a tree surviving war in Jerusalem, or sitting by a pear tree in New York, Haskell brings a sense of reverence and curiosity to each encounter.

His approach bridges science and poetry, data and wonder.

The Voices of Trees

One of the most fascinating aspects of the book is the idea that trees communicate.

Through chemical signals, they can warn neighboring plants of danger. Through their roots and fungal networks, they can share nutrients and information. Through their interactions with animals, they shape the evolution of life.

Even the sound of wind in their branches is part of their story—trees adapt their shapes to sway and survive.

Haskell emphasizes that these forms of communication are not metaphors—they are real, measurable exchanges that science is only beginning to fully understand.

Human Entanglement

The Songs of Trees is not just about forests—it’s about people, too.

Haskell examines how human life is entangled with trees. The pear tree in Manhattan has survived centuries of change, carrying stories of colonization, resilience, and memory.

The olive tree in Jerusalem stands amid conflict, yet continues to offer fruit and shade, transcending human borders.

Haskell reminds us that trees are woven into our histories, economies, and spiritual traditions. They are part of our cities, our homes, our rituals.

Yet, as he points out, we often forget this connection—treating trees as background rather than partners in life.

Resilience and Fragility

Throughout the book, Haskell balances awe with urgency.

Trees are resilient—they endure storms, droughts, and centuries of human activity. But they are also fragile in the face of climate change, deforestation, and pollution.

Urban trees fight to survive amid concrete and traffic. Forest trees face the pressures of logging and warming temperatures.

Haskell’s message is clear: our fates are intertwined. Caring for trees is not a sentimental gesture—it’s essential for the health of our planet and ourselves.

The Ethical Invitation

More than a scientific exploration, The Songs of Trees is an ethical invitation.

It asks us to slow down, to listen, to recognize the living networks we often ignore. It challenges us to see trees not as silent objects, but as beings engaged in dynamic, meaningful lives.

Haskell argues that listening to the songs of trees can guide us toward more compassionate, sustainable ways of living.

It’s not just about preserving forests—it’s about remembering our place within them.

Key Themes and Takeaways

  • Interconnection: Trees are not isolated—they thrive in networks, forming partnerships with fungi, animals, and people.
  • Communication: Trees “speak” through chemical signals, sounds, and shared resources.
  • Cultural Ties: Trees hold deep significance in human history, memory, and tradition.
  • Urban Nature Matters: Trees in cities are vital partners, offering shade, beauty, and resilience amid human structures.
  • Science and Wonder: Data and poetic observation together reveal deeper truths.
  • Shared Fate: The survival of trees and humans is linked; protecting trees is protecting ourselves.
  • Listening as Care: Slowing down to truly observe and listen is a form of respect and connection.

Why This Book Matters

The Songs of Trees invites us to see the living world with fresh eyes.

In an era of ecological crisis and urban disconnection, Haskell offers a path back to intimacy with the more-than-human world.

His writing urges us to listen—to the whisper of wind in the branches, to the hidden signals in the soil, to the stories etched in bark and root.

When we truly hear the songs of trees, we may rediscover something we’ve forgotten: that we are not apart from nature, but deeply embedded within it.
