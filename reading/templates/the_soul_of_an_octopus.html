Day 223: The Soul of an Octopus by Sy Montgomery

Introduction

What does it feel like to be an octopus? Can we ever truly understand a mind so different from our own?

In The Soul of an Octopus, naturalist and author Sy Montgomery dives deep into the mysterious world of these intelligent, shape-shifting creatures. Through hands-on encounters with octopuses in aquariums and the wild, Montgomery explores not just the science of the octopus, but the possibility of interspecies friendship and emotional connection.

Published in 2015, the book is a beautiful blend of memoir, marine biology, and philosophical reflection, inviting readers to question what it means to think, feel, and know across species lines.

Meeting the Octopuses

Montgomery’s journey begins at the New England Aquarium, where she meets her first octopus—Athena. This encounter sparks a fascination that leads her to develop close relationships with several octopuses, including Octavia, Kali, Karma, and Rudy.

Each octopus has a distinct personality. Some are curious and playful; others are shy or mischievous. They recognize individual people, remember them, and show preferences for certain caretakers.

Through these friendships, Montgomery discovers that octopuses are not just fascinating animals—they are beings with intelligence, emotions, and what she comes to describe as “souls.”

The Science of Octopus Intelligence

Montgomery explores the unique biology of octopuses.

They have three hearts, blue blood, and the ability to change color and texture in the blink of an eye. Their bodies are almost entirely muscle, and they can squeeze through spaces as small as a coin.

But what truly astonishes is their distributed intelligence. Two-thirds of an octopus’s neurons are in its arms, meaning each arm can independently taste, touch, and even make decisions.

Despite having completely different brain structures from vertebrates, octopuses display problem-solving abilities, memory, playfulness, and complex behaviors.

They can open jars, solve mazes, and escape enclosures with remarkable skill. They watch and learn from their surroundings, displaying curiosity that rivals that of mammals.

Emotional Lives and Relationships

What sets The Soul of an Octopus apart is Montgomery’s exploration of octopuses as emotional beings.

She describes how Athena would reach out to her with a soft, exploratory touch, and how other octopuses would react to different people with affection, excitement, or even suspicion.

Montgomery suggests that octopuses may feel pleasure, curiosity, fear, and even something like affection. She doesn’t claim to fully understand their emotions, but she believes these animals experience the world in rich, conscious ways.

Through her time with the aquarium staff and other volunteers, she builds a community of people who are equally captivated by the possibility of emotional connection with these creatures.

The Mystery of Consciousness

The book raises profound philosophical questions: How do we define consciousness? Can a creature with no bones, with a nervous system radically different from ours, possess a sense of self?

Montgomery acknowledges the scientific challenges of answering these questions but insists that the octopus’s behavior cannot be dismissed as simple instinct.

Instead, she argues that the octopus invites us to expand our understanding of intelligence and to accept that minds can come in forms very different from our own.

Her encounters suggest that consciousness may not be a human privilege but something that has evolved in surprising and diverse ways throughout the animal kingdom.

Life, Death, and the Shortness of Time

One of the most moving aspects of The Soul of an Octopus is its exploration of life’s brevity.

Octopuses live astonishingly short lives—often only three to five years. Despite their intelligence and emotional depth, their time on Earth is fleeting.

Montgomery wrestles with this reality, witnessing the deaths of the octopuses she comes to know and care for. These moments are tender, sad, and ultimately humbling.

Through these experiences, she reflects on the nature of grief, the impermanence of life, and the preciousness of time—whether human or octopus.

Diving into the Wild

Montgomery’s fascination eventually takes her beyond the aquarium to scuba diving trips where she observes wild octopuses in their natural habitats.

In the wild, octopuses are masters of camouflage, skilled hunters, and agile explorers of the ocean floor.

These dives reinforce her belief that octopuses are not just clever animals—they are sentient beings navigating rich and complex lives beneath the waves.

Key Themes and Takeaways

  • Interspecies Connection: The possibility of friendship, recognition, and emotional exchange between humans and octopuses.
  • Unique Intelligence: Octopuses display advanced problem-solving, learning, memory, and curiosity despite having very different neural structures.
  • The Mystery of Consciousness: The book challenges the idea that complex consciousness is exclusive to vertebrates.
  • Life’s Fragility: Octopuses live short but vibrant lives, reminding us to cherish our own fleeting time.
  • Empathy Across Species: By observing and respecting animals unlike ourselves, we can expand our capacity for empathy and understanding.
  • The Beauty of Curiosity: Montgomery’s journey is driven by wonder—a willingness to explore the unknown and ask difficult questions.

Why This Book Matters

The Soul of an Octopus is a heart-opening exploration of intelligence, emotion, and connection beyond human boundaries.

Sy Montgomery’s storytelling is personal, tender, and deeply curious. She brings the octopus out of the realm of the strange and into the realm of the familiar—without diminishing its mystery.

The book invites us to rethink what it means to have a mind, to feel, to be alive.

It suggests that the world is far richer, more complex, and more interconnected than we often allow ourselves to believe.

Through her encounters, Montgomery reminds us that wonder is not just for the exotic—it is everywhere, if only we choose to see.
