Day 229: The Story of More by Hope Jahren

Introduction

The Story of More: How We Got to Climate Change and Where to Go from Here by Hope Jahren is a clear, personal, and accessible guide to understanding the modern climate crisis. Published in 2020, the book focuses on the patterns of consumption and growth that have driven environmental change over the past century.

Jahren, a renowned geobiologist and author of Lab Girl, brings a unique voice to the climate conversation—one that combines scientific rigor with deep personal reflection. Through relatable comparisons and global data, she invites readers to see how the small, everyday choices we make are tied to larger global systems.

How We Got Here: The Story of More

Jahren begins by tracing the rapid changes that have shaped our world over the past 50 years. Humanity has grown in number, in wealth, and in appetite.

From the mid-20th century, we have seen exponential increases in energy consumption, agricultural production, and material use. We produce more food, use more water, and burn more fuel than ever before.

This growth has not been evenly distributed. People in high-income countries consume far more resources per person than those in low-income nations.

Jahren highlights the scale of inequality: some people live in abundance, while others live with scarcity. Yet, regardless of geography, everyone is affected by the changes in the Earth’s systems.

Energy, Food, and Waste

Jahren carefully unpacks the major drivers of environmental degradation, starting with energy.

The energy we use today—whether for heating homes, powering cars, or running factories—mostly comes from fossil fuels. Burning coal, oil, and natural gas releases carbon dioxide, the primary driver of global warming.

Jahren emphasizes that our modern conveniences—electricity, air travel, fast transportation—come with an invisible cost to the planet.

She also explores agriculture, showing how modern farming methods have increased yields but also led to soil depletion, deforestation, and water overuse.

We produce more meat, more sugar, and more packaged foods than previous generations. At the same time, we waste enormous amounts of food—while millions of people remain hungry.

The story of more is also the story of waste. From plastics clogging oceans to the byproducts of energy production, our waste lingers long after consumption ends.

Climate Change: The Consequence of More

The result of this relentless expansion is climate change.

Jahren explains the science simply: carbon dioxide traps heat in the atmosphere, and as we burn more fossil fuels, we increase this heat-trapping layer.

The evidence is all around us—rising temperatures, melting glaciers, stronger storms, and shifting ecosystems.

But Jahren avoids the doom-heavy tone of many climate discussions. Instead, she emphasizes that the changes we face are the result of choices—choices we can understand, question, and ultimately change.

The Power of Less

One of the core ideas in The Story of More is that individual action matters.

Jahren does not call for perfection. She does not ask readers to abandon technology or return to a primitive way of life.

Instead, she advocates for “less.”

Less waste. Less energy. Less meat. Less mindless consumption.

She shows how reducing personal consumption, even in small ways, can contribute to meaningful change when multiplied across millions of people.

Jahren argues that every bit of “less” slows the pace of climate change.

Why We Struggle to Change

Despite the clear science, many societies continue down the path of “more.”

Jahren explores why change is difficult. Habit, comfort, economic systems, and social norms all push us toward consumption.

We are bombarded by marketing that tells us we need more—more food, more gadgets, more luxury.

But she emphasizes that happiness does not necessarily come from more. Instead, connection, purpose, and sustainability can offer deeper fulfillment.

Jahren also notes that the people most affected by climate change are often the least responsible for it. This raises profound questions of fairness and justice.

Hope and Collective Action

Despite the scale of the problem, Jahren is hopeful.

She believes that humans are capable of empathy, cooperation, and innovation. She points to historical examples of societal change—how people have come together to improve public health, end wars, and confront injustice.

Jahren emphasizes the power of education, especially for young people. She encourages readers to talk about climate change, to stay informed, and to push for policy changes that prioritize sustainability.

She also reminds us that climate change is not a single problem with a single solution. It is a mosaic of challenges that can be addressed from many directions—through science, art, politics, and everyday choices.

Key Themes and Takeaways

  • The Story of More: Overconsumption and exponential growth have driven climate change.
  • Unequal Impact: Wealthy countries consume far more resources, while the poorest suffer the most.
  • Energy and Waste: Fossil fuels power our lives but create lasting environmental damage.
  • Food Systems: Modern agriculture has improved yields but caused significant harm to ecosystems.
  • Climate Change is Us: The climate crisis is the cumulative result of billions of small choices.
  • Less is Powerful: Reducing personal consumption is a meaningful and accessible step.
  • Hope is Action: We can choose to act, to educate, and to advocate for systemic change.

Why This Book Matters

The Story of More stands out because it speaks directly to the everyday person.

Hope Jahren does not lecture or overwhelm with technical jargon. She patiently explains how ordinary life—what we eat, how we travel, what we buy—is connected to the global climate.

She acknowledges that change is hard but reminds us that doing something is far better than doing nothing.

This book empowers readers to start with small, manageable steps and to believe in the possibility of larger, collective action.

It shows that the story of more does not have to be the story of forever.

We can write a new story—one of balance, sufficiency, and care for the Earth and each other.
