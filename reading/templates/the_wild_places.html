Day 234: The Wild Places by Robert Macfarlane

Introduction

The Wild Places by Robert Macfarlane, published in 2007, is a poetic and meditative journey across the remaining wild landscapes of Britain and Ireland. Blending travel writing, natural history, personal reflection, and deep philosophical inquiry, Macfarlane’s book asks a fundamental question: in an age of roads, cities, and human expansion, do true wild places still exist?

His answer is both surprising and deeply moving. Through forests, moors, saltmarshes, islands, rivers, and mountaintops, Macfarlane explores the many forms of wilderness—revealing that wildness is not confined to vast, untouched frontiers. It is often closer than we think.

A Journey Across Britain and Ireland

Macfarlane sets out to seek the wild in a landscape often considered tamed and domesticated. Inspired by the writing of earlier nature explorers like Roger Deakin and Nan Shepherd, he begins his quest with the belief that wildness exists only in remote, rugged places—untouched by human hands.

His journey takes him from the windswept coasts of Scotland to the saltmarshes of East Anglia, from ancient woodlands to high mountain ridges. Along the way, he climbs trees, swims in cold rivers, sleeps under the stars, and spends nights alone in ancient forests. But as he travels, his understanding of wildness begins to shift.

The Myth of Pristine Wilderness

One of the central ideas in The Wild Places is the challenge to the notion that wilderness must be vast, distant, and untouched. Macfarlane discovers that true wildness often exists in small, overlooked corners—in a hedgerow, a city park, a patch of old woodland.

He writes that wildness is not about remoteness, but about vitality. It is about places that resist control, that harbor life beyond human design. Even in familiar landscapes, the wild persists.

This realization transforms his journey. He begins to see wildness not as something lost, but as something still present, woven into the fabric of everyday life.

A Deep Connection to Place

Throughout the book, Macfarlane’s writing reveals his profound connection to the landscapes he visits. He does not simply describe them—he enters them, physically and emotionally.

He writes about the feeling of walking across frozen lakes, the texture of ancient stones, the sound of wind moving through trees. Each place holds layers of history, myth, and memory. Macfarlane draws on local stories, old maps, and the words of past writers to bring the land alive.

His detailed, sensory descriptions immerse the reader in the experience of being there—feeling the cold, hearing the birds, seeing the shifting light.

Solitude, Risk, and Wonder

Macfarlane often travels alone, seeking solitude in his encounters with the wild. There are moments of real physical challenge—spending cold nights outdoors, traversing dangerous ridges, swimming in rough seas.

But these experiences are not about conquering nature. They are about stepping outside the predictable rhythms of modern life, about opening oneself to wonder.

Macfarlane’s writing captures the vulnerability and humility that come with being in the wild. His journeys remind us that risk is part of what makes wilderness meaningful. The unpredictability of the wild keeps us honest, present, and attentive.

A Changing View of Wildness

By the end of his travels, Macfarlane’s understanding of wildness has transformed. Initially, he believed that true wilderness was vanishing, that it could only be found in remote places.

But he comes to see that wildness is not a fixed location. It is a quality, a presence that can exist even in human-shaped landscapes.

It exists in the way brambles reclaim abandoned buildings, in the migratory paths of birds over cities, in the way rivers cut through farmlands. Wildness, he realizes, is not something outside us—it is something we are part of.

Themes and Reflections

  • Wildness is everywhere: It is not limited to distant, untouched lands. It is present in the local, the small, the familiar.
  • Nature resists control: Even in managed landscapes, life finds ways to flourish unpredictably.
  • Physical immersion matters: Walking, climbing, swimming—engaging the body in place brings us closer to the wild.
  • Solitude is valuable: Time alone in wild places fosters deep reflection and connection.
  • History and myth enrich place: Landscapes are layered with stories that deepen our relationship to them.
  • Risk and vulnerability are essential: They heighten our awareness and respect for the natural world.

Why This Book Matters

The Wild Places is more than a travelogue—it’s a meditation on the meaning of wilderness in the modern world.

Macfarlane’s lyrical prose invites us to rethink what we consider wild, and to find beauty and mystery in places we might otherwise overlook. His writing challenges the assumption that wilderness is always distant, always disappearing. Instead, he reveals it as something present and enduring.

This book encourages us to slow down, to look closer, to walk outside our familiar paths, and to rediscover the wild that exists alongside us.

Macfarlane’s journey ultimately offers hope. Wildness is not gone. It is waiting—patiently, quietly—in trees, rivers, shorelines, and city edges. And by seeking it, we don’t just find the world. We find ourselves.
