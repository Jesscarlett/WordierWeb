Day 235: Underland by Robert Macfarlane

Introduction

Underland: A Deep Time Journey by Robert Macfarlane, published in 2019, is a profound exploration of the worlds beneath our feet. Known for his lyrical writing and philosophical depth, Macfarlane takes readers on an extraordinary descent—into caves, catacombs, glaciers, tree roots, and burial chambers—seeking to understand the hidden spaces that shape our world.

The book is a meditation on deep time, on the traces we leave behind, and on the interwoven histories of humans and the subterranean. It challenges us to look downward and to consider what lies below, both physically and metaphorically.

A Journey into the Underworld

Macfarlane frames his journey as a descent—a classic literary structure echoing mythological tales from Orpheus to Dante.

But this is not just a symbolic journey. It is a physical one. He travels across Europe, entering underground rivers in Italy, descending sea caves in Norway, crawling through ancient mines in England, and even venturing beneath the ice in Greenland.

Each underground encounter reveals different facets of our relationship with the Earth. Some are places of concealment—nuclear waste storage facilities designed to last longer than civilizations. Some are places of discovery—deep caves that preserve ancient art and human history. Some are places of passage—networks of tunnels beneath cities that mirror life above.

The Three Types of Underground

Macfarlane categorizes the underland in three ways:

  • As a place to shelter: Humans have long used caves and tunnels to hide from danger and store precious things.
  • As a place to dispose: We bury the dead, toxic waste, and even ideas we wish to forget.
  • As a place to explore: Scientists, cavers, and explorers continue to descend in search of knowledge, beauty, and mystery.

Throughout the book, these categories intertwine, raising ethical questions about what we choose to place underground and what responsibilities we have to future generations.

Deep Time and Human Traces

A central theme in Underland is the concept of deep time—geological time that stretches over millions and billions of years.

Macfarlane is fascinated by how the underground preserves evidence of both ancient events and recent human actions. In deep caves, he finds paintings that have survived for tens of thousands of years. In glacial ice, he sees layers that record past climates. In nuclear waste repositories, he confronts the sobering reality that we are creating materials that will remain hazardous for hundreds of thousands of years.

These moments force him—and us—to consider our place in time. What legacies will we leave for the distant future? How will the marks we make on the Earth be interpreted by those who come after us?

Stories from Beneath

Macfarlane’s journeys introduce us to a remarkable cast of people:

  • Tree scientists in Slovenia who study underground networks of roots.
  • Urban explorers who navigate hidden rivers and forgotten bunkers beneath cities.
  • Arctic researchers who witness the melting of ancient ice.
  • French cataphiles who roam the labyrinthine catacombs beneath Paris.

Each story illuminates a different aspect of the underworld, from scientific discovery to philosophical reflection. Macfarlane blends adventure, history, science, and myth in his storytelling, creating a narrative that feels both intimate and expansive.

The Wood Wide Web

One of the most fascinating parts of the book explores the underground fungal networks that connect trees—what scientists call the "wood wide web."

Macfarlane visits a forest in Slovenia where researchers study how trees communicate and share resources through these networks. It is a revelation: forests are not collections of isolated individuals but interconnected communities.

This underground cooperation challenges dominant ideas about competition in nature and offers a powerful metaphor for human relationships.

Environmental Reckoning

As with his other works, Macfarlane does not shy away from confronting environmental crises. The book’s journey beneath the ice in Greenland is a chilling reminder of climate change. The ice is melting faster than expected, revealing deep crevasses and ancient secrets—but also signaling profound loss.

The exploration of nuclear waste storage in Finland raises haunting questions about how to warn future civilizations of hidden dangers buried deep underground.

Macfarlane reminds us that the decisions we make today—what we bury, what we destroy, what we choose to preserve—will ripple through deep time.

Key Themes and Takeaways

  • The underground is a place of shelter, disposal, and exploration.
  • Deep time challenges our sense of scale and responsibility.
  • Human traces, from cave art to nuclear waste, will outlast us.
  • The underground is rich with stories, histories, and connections.
  • Forests are interconnected through vast underground networks.
  • Climate change is rapidly reshaping even the deepest layers of Earth.
  • What we hide beneath the surface reveals much about who we are.

Why This Book Matters

Underland is a masterful exploration of the hidden layers of the world. It invites readers to descend—physically, intellectually, and emotionally—into spaces we rarely consider.

Robert Macfarlane’s writing is beautiful, thoughtful, and deeply immersive. He moves effortlessly between adventure and philosophy, science and poetry.

This book matters because it shifts our perspective. It asks us to look downward, to think beyond our lifespans, and to question the legacies we leave behind.

It is a story about what lies beneath, but it is also a story about us—our fears, our hopes, our responsibilities.

Underland reminds us that the past is written in stone, in ice, in hidden chambers—and that the future is already being carved beneath our feet.
