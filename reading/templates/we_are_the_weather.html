Day 113: We Are the Weather by Jonathan Safran Foer

Introduction

Jonathan Safran Foer’s We Are the Weather: Saving the Planet Begins at Breakfast is an urgent and unconventional call to action in the fight against climate change. Published in 2019, the book doesn’t focus on new technologies or political reforms—it focuses on the choices we make every day, particularly regarding food. Foer, known for his emotionally driven and narrative-rich nonfiction, presents a compelling argument that the most effective way individuals can combat climate change is by changing what we eat, especially by reducing our consumption of animal products.

What sets this book apart is its deeply personal tone. It is not just a book about the environment; it is also about our struggles with willpower, identity, and the dissonance between knowing what’s right and actually doing it. With humor, honesty, and philosophical depth, Foer blends science, memoir, and moral argumentation to show how small, consistent changes in lifestyle can collectively make a monumental impact.

Summary

Foer’s central thesis is simple but powerful: if we want to mitigate climate change, we need to radically shift our diets—especially in the wealthier nations. He emphasizes that agriculture, particularly animal agriculture, is one of the leading contributors to greenhouse gas emissions, deforestation, and biodiversity loss.

  • The Facts We Ignore – Foer opens with the science: climate change is real, urgent, and largely caused by human activity. He presents clear data on methane, carbon emissions, and the environmental cost of meat and dairy production.
  • The Disconnect Between Knowledge and Action – The author delves into why we fail to act despite overwhelming evidence. He reflects on human psychology, denial, and the difficulty of making personal sacrifices.
  • Diet as a Moral Choice – He argues that the most powerful form of climate activism isn’t in protest or policy—it’s in what we eat, especially avoiding animal products before dinner.
  • Collective Action Begins with Individuals – While acknowledging that systemic change is necessary, Foer asserts that individual choices create momentum, build cultural norms, and put pressure on institutions.
  • Stories and Struggles – Through personal anecdotes, historical parallels (like WWII mobilization), and family reflections, Foer brings emotional weight to the intellectual case for action.

The book ends with a challenge: will we act not just for ourselves, but for those who come after us?

Memorable Insight or Case Study

One of the book’s most memorable sections compares climate change to the existential threat posed by Nazi Germany during WWII. Foer recalls how ordinary people made sacrifices—like rationing, working in factories, or enlisting—not because they were easy, but because they were necessary. He uses this analogy to argue that confronting climate change requires a similar level of collective commitment and moral clarity.

Key Study Points

  • Animal Agriculture is a Major Climate Driver – Reducing meat and dairy is one of the most impactful personal actions.
  • Knowledge Alone is Not Enough – Behavioral change requires emotional, cultural, and communal motivation.
  • Individual Choices Matter – Collective action is built on many individual decisions.
  • Change is Hard—but Necessary – Action is often uncomfortable but morally imperative.
  • We Are the Problem and the Solution – Our lifestyles cause the crisis, but also contain its resolution.

Key Concepts and Explanation

  • Before Dinner Pledge – Foer proposes avoiding animal products before dinner as a manageable, effective commitment.
    Significance: Even partial shifts can yield big environmental benefits.
  • Moral Imagination – The idea that we must envision a better future and act accordingly, even if it's uncomfortable now.
    Example: Parents making sacrifices for their children’s wellbeing.
  • Climate Denial is Emotional, Not Intellectual – Many accept the facts but still behave as if they’re not true.
    Relevance: Points to the need for culture shifts, not just more information.
  • Systemic Change Through Cultural Change – Governments and corporations follow public will; personal choices influence larger systems.

Quotes

“Saving the planet requires us to change how we live—starting with how we eat.”
“We cannot be both consumers and citizens. We must choose.”

Fun or Surprising Fact

According to Foer, if every American skipped eating animal products for just one meal a day, it would be like taking 27 million cars off the road. This powerful statistic reframes dietary change as a form of climate activism.

Real-Life Application

Foer’s message encourages readers to start small: skip meat for breakfast and lunch, choose plant-based alternatives, and talk to others about sustainable food. It also empowers people to see that their habits—though mundane—are deeply political and planetary in scale.

Recommended Books

  • Eating Animals by Jonathan Safran Foer – A precursor to this book that dives deeper into animal ethics and the food industry.
  • Animal, Vegetable, Miracle by Barbara Kingsolver – Explores seasonal, local eating and its environmental and personal benefits.
