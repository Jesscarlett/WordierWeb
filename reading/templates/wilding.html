Day 214: Wilding by Isabella Tree

Introduction

What happens when we stop trying to control nature—and instead, let it take the lead?

In Wilding, journalist and conservationist Isabella Tree tells the extraordinary story of the Knepp Estate in West Sussex, England. Once a struggling, intensively farmed property, Knepp was transformed through a radical experiment: abandoning traditional agriculture and allowing nature to reclaim the land.

The result is one of the most ambitious and successful rewilding projects in Europe—a living example of how biodiversity can rebound when human interference is dialed back.

Part memoir, part ecological exploration, Wilding is a powerful call to rethink our relationship with the land—and to trust in the resilience of nature.

The Problem with Modern Farming

Before its transformation, the Knepp Estate was like many other British farms. It was run with increasing reliance on chemicals, machinery, and government subsidies—but profits were declining and the soil was deteriorating.

Tree and her husband, Charles Burrell, faced a difficult choice: continue struggling with intensive farming, or do something radically different.

After years of financial and ecological strain, they decided to step away. To let go. And to hand over the reins to nature.

Their decision was met with skepticism and criticism. Neighbors scoffed. Experts doubted them. But they held firm.

The Philosophy of Rewilding

At its heart, rewilding is about allowing natural processes to re-establish themselves. It’s not just “letting things go wild”—it’s reintroducing lost species, removing barriers, and creating the conditions for ecosystems to evolve freely.

At Knepp, this meant releasing free-roaming animals—longhorn cattle, Tamworth pigs, Exmoor ponies, and red and fallow deer—to mimic the role of ancient herbivores in shaping landscapes.

It meant letting scrub and thorn grow, allowing natural succession to unfold without human pruning or planting. It also meant patience—something sorely lacking in many conservation efforts.

Wohlleben’s central thesis in his tree books echoes here: the less we interfere, the more robust nature becomes.

Biodiversity Bounces Back

What followed was astonishing.

Rare species began to appear—many for the first time in generations. The elusive purple emperor butterfly returned. Nightingales, turtle doves, and peregrine falcons began nesting. The soil, once compacted and degraded, teemed with life.

Even more remarkable were the increases in invertebrate diversity, fungi, and wildflowers. Where once there were monocultures of wheat or barley, now stood diverse, self-regulating ecosystems.

Knepp became a magnet for ecologists, ornithologists, and conservationists from around the world. It proved that ecosystems can heal—if we give them space to breathe.

Cultural Resistance and Public Skepticism

Not everyone celebrated the change.

Many locals were disturbed by the “messy” appearance of the land. Others worried about ticks, invasive species, or the absence of crops. Critics argued that the approach was nostalgic or even elitist.

But Tree addresses these concerns head-on, explaining that neatness is not a measure of ecological health. In fact, many of our manicured landscapes are biological deserts.

She urges readers to let go of tidy lawns, rigid order, and the illusion of control—and to embrace the beauty of complexity and unpredictability.

Lessons from the Wild

One of the central takeaways from Wilding is that nature knows best.

When given the opportunity, ecosystems self-organize in ways we couldn’t design ourselves. Tree recounts how beavers (once extinct in Britain) reshape waterways with engineering precision. How pigs help create microhabitats by rooting in the soil. How deer grazing patterns prevent ecological stagnation.

Rewilding doesn’t mean doing nothing. It means making thoughtful interventions—reintroducing missing species, removing harmful infrastructure, and then stepping back.

The wild does the rest.

A Vision for the Future

Tree makes a passionate case for scaling up rewilding across the UK and beyond. Not to replace all farming, but to rethink land use—especially on marginal or degraded land.

She argues for a new model of coexistence, where food production, conservation, and human culture can flourish side by side. She envisions corridors of rewilded land connecting fragmented habitats, allowing species to move and adapt as the climate changes.

Rewilding, in her view, is not a luxury—it’s a necessity. A practical, hopeful response to biodiversity loss, climate change, and ecological collapse.

Quotes to Remember

“Nature is not neat. It is wild, messy, dynamic, unpredictable—and astonishingly resilient.”
“The key to rewilding is not control, but letting go.”
“What we discovered at Knepp is that nature can heal—if only we let it.”

Why This Book Matters

In a world increasingly shaped by human hands, Wilding is a powerful reminder that letting go can be an act of stewardship.

It shows that we don’t always need to plant, plan, or manage. Sometimes, the most radical and regenerative thing we can do is to step back—and watch life return.

Tree’s writing is eloquent, informed, and deeply inspiring. She blends personal narrative with ecology, history, and policy, making a compelling case for a future where people and the planet thrive together.

Whether you’re a farmer, a conservationist, or simply someone who loves the natural world, Wilding will leave you rethinking what it means to care for the Earth.
